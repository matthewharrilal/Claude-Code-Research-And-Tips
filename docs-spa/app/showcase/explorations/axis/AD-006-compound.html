The Development Kitchen

Not every compound combination works. These are the experiments that informed the final pattern selection.

Z-Pattern + Spiral (experimental)

Attempting to nest a spiral within a Z-pattern quadrant creates competing reading paths. The Z diagonal conflicts with the spiral's inward motion. Result: Disorienting. The eye cannot serve two masters simultaneously.

Lesson: Compound patterns work across sections, not within them. Each section commits to one axis.

Bento + Choreography (experimental)

Bento grid with a hub-spoke center cell. The hub wants to radiate outward; the grid wants equal-weight cells. These goals conflict when the featured cell must simultaneously be “equal” (grid citizen) and “central” (hub).

Lesson: Bento features emerge from size differentiation. Choreography features emerge from connection count. Combining them requires a clear primary: either Bento with choreographic connections, or Choreography with bento-sized spokes.

All Five Simultaneous (experimental)

Attempting all five patterns in a single viewport creates visual cacophony. The section-by-section approach (used in this page) emerged as the correct solution: each section commits to one axis, and the compound effect emerges from the sequence, not from simultaneous application.

Lesson: Compound IS sequential, not simultaneous. The patterns layer through time (scrolling) not space (viewport).
