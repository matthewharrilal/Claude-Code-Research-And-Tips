Two-Layer Scoring

A framework for auditing practitioner documentation

You've been there. The documentation is comprehensiveâ€”every feature documented, every API explained, every edge case noted. Yet developers still struggle. They know what things are, but can't figure out how to become someone who uses them.

The problem isn't the content. The problem is the journey architecture.

Traditional documentation audits focus on a single dimension: Is the information accurate? Is it complete? These matter, but they're only part of the story. You can have perfect content and still fail your readers.

* * *

The Two-Layer Scoring System splits evaluation into two weighted components:

Layer 1 (40%): Content Quality

Mental models, actionability, source fidelity, coherence, completeness. The "what is this?" dimension.

Layer 2 (60%): User Journey

Orientation, motivation, procedures, checkpoints, failure handling. The "how do I succeed?" dimension.

The 40/60 split isn't arbitrary. In auditing 100+ files of practitioner documentation, we found that content quality problems are easier to fix. Missing a section? Add it. Inaccurate quote? Verify it. But journey problems require structural rethinking. That's why journey gets more weightâ€”it's where the hard work lives.

This insight reframes everything. Documentation isn't a knowledge repositoryâ€”it's a transformation engine. Readers don't want to know things. They want to become people who can do things.

The scoring rubric captures this by asking journey questions that content audits miss:

  • Orientation: Does the user know where they are and what they need?
  • Checkpoints: Can users verify they're on track before continuing?
  • Failure: When things go wrong, is recovery documented?

Most documentation scores 80% on content quality but under 50% on journey readiness. That gap is why readers struggle despite comprehensive coverage.

* * *

There's a counterintuitive finding buried in this framework: the Expertise Reversal Effect. Instructional techniques that work for beginners can actually harm expert learning.

Progressive disclosure slows experts down. Verbose explanations force artificial mental decomposition. "Beginner-friendly" structure creates friction for senior practitioners. For practitioner documentation, dense reference beats progressive disclosure. Copy-paste ready beats explained concepts.

This doesn't mean abandoning beginners. It means understanding your audience and optimizing for their actual usage patterns, not assumed ones.
