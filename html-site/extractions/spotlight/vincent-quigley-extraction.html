Essence (15 words)
Treat AI like a junior developer who resets each morning. Review everything. Budget three attempts.

Vincent Quigley is a Staff Software Engineer at Sanity. After 18 months with Cursor and 6 weeks with Claude Code, he developed a mental model that cuts through the hype: AI is a junior developer who never learns between sessions.

"First attempt will be 95% garbage."

2 The Core Abstraction

The Mental Model
"Treating AI like a 'junior developer who doesn't learn' became my mental model for success."
This framing provides realistic expectations, appropriate workflow, and sustainable approach. It transforms frustration into patience.

What a Junior Developer Does

Behavior Junior Dev AI Agent
Needs clear instructions
Makes mistakes regularly
Requires code review
Learns from feedback (in session)
Learns between sessions NEVER
Is eager and fast
Can be surprisingly good

The key difference: The "doesn't learn" part. Every session starts fresh. Every correction must be re-taught. The cost of context loss is permanent.

The Three-Attempt Reality

"Expecting perfection on attempt one is like expecting a junior developer to nail a complex feature without context."
Expected Output Quality by Attempt
1
Attempt 1: 95% Garbage
Purpose: Claude builds context; you identify challenges. Don't expect usable code. Value: You now understand what's hard about this problem.
2
Attempt 2: 50% Garbage
Purpose: AI understands nuances from Attempt 1. Core logic is taking shape. Half is still unusable, but the foundation is forming.
3
Attempt 3: Workable (~80% useful)
Purpose: Produce an iterable foundation. Not a final product, but a starting point you can refine productively.

3 Design Decisions

WHY "JUNIOR DEVELOPER" FRAMING?
The framing sets expectations correctly. You wouldn't rage-quit when a junior makes mistakes. You'd guide them, review their work, and expect iteration. The same patience applies to AI.
What this means for you
Treat AI output like a junior's work: review thoroughly, expect to iterate, don't expect architectural insights. YOUR job is architecture and judgment. AI's job is implementation velocity.
WHY THREE ATTEMPTS, NOT ONE?
First attempts reveal the problem, not solve it. The AI's initial output shows you what's hard about this task. Attempt 2 incorporates your clarifications. Attempt 3 reaches workable.
What this means for you
Budget time for iteration, not perfection. If you expect one-shot success, you'll be frustrated. If you expect three attempts, you'll be pleasantly surprised when it works faster.
WHY "2-3x NOT 10x" PRODUCTIVITY?
Anti-hype calibration prevents disappointment. Social media amplifies success stories and ignores iteration costs. Real productivity is 2-3x faster, which is still transformative.
What this means for you
Set realistic expectations. Measure accurately. 2-3x faster at $1,000-1,500/month still has excellent ROI. The hype of "10x" leads to disappointment; the reality of "2-3x" leads to sustainable adoption.
WHY THREE-STEP REVIEW?
AI review catches mechanical issues. Engineer review catches architectural issues. Team review maintains quality bar. Each layer catches different problems.
What this means for you
Never skip the engineer review step. AI can't judge architectural fit or business requirements. "AI reviewed it" is not enough. You must review it. Then your team must review it.
WHY MARK HUMAN EDITS?
AI will overwrite your manual changes without hesitation. It doesn't know which code is "yours" vs "generated." Explicit markers protect manual work.
What this means for you
Use comments like // HUMAN EDIT - do not modify to protect critical manual work. Without markers, expect your careful changes to be silently overwritten.

5 The Process

The Three-Attempt Process

Attempt 1: Context Building (expect 95% garbage)
1
Give task to Claude
Describe what you want. Don't over-specify.
2
Let it work through initial approach
Watch what it does. Note what it gets wrong.
3
DON'T expect usable code
This attempt is for learning, not shipping.
4
DO note what's hard about this problem
Output: Understanding of problem, not solution. Time: ~15-30 minutes.
Attempt 2: Core Logic (expect 50% garbage)
1
Provide feedback from Attempt 1
Specify what was wrong. Clarify requirements that emerged.
2
Let Claude iterate
Core logic should be taking shape. Output: Core structure, still needs work. Time: ~30-45 minutes.
Attempt 3: Workable Foundation
1
Incorporate learnings from Attempts 1 and 2
Focus on getting to "good enough to refine."
2
Accept ~80% useful, ~20% to fix
Move to review phase. Output: Not final product, but solid starting point. Time: ~20-30 minutes.

The Three-Step Review Process

Step 1: Claude Auto-Review

Prompt: "Review this code for bugs, test gaps, and style"

  • Test coverage gaps
  • Obvious bugs
  • Style violations
  • Missing error handling

Why: Catches mechanical issues cheaply.

Step 2: Engineer Review (YOU)
  • Architecture appropriateness
  • Business logic correctness
  • Maintainability
  • Edge case handling

Why: AI can't judge architectural fit or business requirements.

Step 3: Normal Team Review
  • Standard code review process
  • Quality bar unchanged from pre-AI
  • Same as any other PR

Why: AI-generated code gets same scrutiny as human code.

7 Gotchas

10 common pitfalls when applying the junior developer mental model:

1. Expecting 10x productivity
Warning sign: Frustrated when AI feels "slow"
Calibration: Reality is 2-3x, still valuable. Adjust expectations, not the tool.
2. First attempt disappointment
Warning sign: "This is terrible" reaction to Attempt 1
Calibration: Expected - it's context building. The value is learning what's hard.
3. No learning between sessions
Warning sign: Same mistake twice in different sessions
Calibration: Capture learnings in CLAUDE.md. The filesystem IS the memory.
4. Confident but wrong code
Warning sign: "Looks right" leads to bugs in production
Calibration: Always verify, never trust. Three-step review is non-negotiable.
5. Skipping review steps
Warning sign: "AI reviewed it" as justification
Calibration: Three-step is three-step. AI review is Step 1, not the whole process.
6. Same problem in parallel instances
Warning sign: Conflicting outputs from multiple Claude sessions
Calibration: Different problem spaces only. Track externally.
7. AI overwrites human edits
Warning sign: Your careful changes disappear
Calibration: Mark with // HUMAN EDIT - do not modify
8. Context window overflow
Warning sign: Degrading output quality in long sessions
Calibration: Break into smaller chunks. Fresh context beats extended sessions.
9. Cost surprise
Warning sign: $1,500+ unexpected bill
Calibration: Budget $1,000-1,500/month for heavy use. Monitor and adjust.
10. Treating AI as senior
Warning sign: Expecting architectural decisions from AI
Calibration: YOU make architecture calls. AI implements. That's the division of labor.

8 What's Hard

These are fundamental tensions, not bugs to fix:

Memory Doesn't Persist
Why it's hard: Architecture limitation. Context resets between sessions. There's no "learning" in the traditional sense.
Coping strategy: External state (CLAUDE.md, MCP integrations). The filesystem IS the memory. Treat CLAUDE.md as institutional knowledge.
Confident but Wrong
Why it's hard: AI has no built-in self-doubt. It produces code with equal confidence whether correct or broken.
Coping strategy: Always verify. Never trust without checking. Human review is essential, not optional.
Can't See Quality
Why it's hard: AI doesn't know good from bad in context. It can't judge architectural fit or maintainability.
Coping strategy: Human review at Step 2 is where quality is enforced. AI can check syntax; you must check sense.
Cost is Real
Why it's hard: Heavy use = $1,000-1,500/month. This is real money that requires ROI calculation.
Coping strategy: Budget, monitor, and do the math. 2-3x productivity at this cost is still excellent ROI for most teams.
Hype vs Reality Gap
Why it's hard: Social media amplifies success stories and ignores iteration costs. The gap between hype and reality causes disappointment.
Coping strategy: Calibrate to 2-3x, not 10x. Honest assessment beats inflated expectations.
Learning Curve
Why it's hard: It takes time to work with AI effectively. The "messy first months" are real.
Coping strategy: Budget for learning. Start with small features. Expect iteration. Get better over time.
"AI can't retain learning between sessions (unless you spend the time manually giving it the 'memories')."
