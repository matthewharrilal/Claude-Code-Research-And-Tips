3 Design Decisions

WHY DOMAIN EXPERTISE FIRST?
"It takes years to learn and ground ideas." AI can implement concepts, but it cannot replace the understanding required to know WHAT to build. The expertise creates the prompt. Without it, you build the wrong thing fast.
What this means for you
Invest in understanding before AI assistance. The time spent learning is not waste - it's the investment that makes AI useful. If you can't explain the problem in 3 paragraphs, you don't understand it well enough yet.
WHY JUST 3 PARAGRAPHS?
The 3-paragraph constraint forces conceptual clarity. If you need 10 pages, you're over-specifying implementation details. If you can't do 3 paragraphs, you don't understand the problem yet. The constraint is a test.
What this means for you
Practice articulating complex problems simply. If Claude asks many clarifying questions, your articulation needs work. The 3-paragraph format: Context (why, what's broken), Requirements (constraints, scale), Behavior (trigger-response, invariants).
WHY IS "TOY VERSION" VALUABLE?
The prototype validates the concept, reveals unknowns, and enables rapid iteration. It's not meant to be production code. It's meant to answer: "Is this the right direction?" Jaana's toy version enabled team alignment discussions that a year of meetings couldn't.
What this means for you
First version is for learning, not production. Budget for productionization as a separate phase. The prototype surfaces the 20% of edge cases that require 80% of the production effort.
WHY GIVE CREDIT TO A COMPETITOR?
"This industry has never been a zero-sum game, so it's easy to give credit where it's due even when it's a competitor." Jaana, a Google engineer, publicly praised Anthropic's Claude Code. This intellectual honesty is a signal of credibility.
What this means for you
Follow Jaana's example. Evaluate tools honestly, not tribally. The best practitioners use the best tools regardless of corporate loyalty. Anti-hype is as misleading as hype.
WHY NOT SKIP THE YEAR?
Without the year of understanding, you can't write the 3-paragraph prompt. You'd ask Claude to "build an agent orchestrator" and get a generic solution that misses all your domain-specific requirements. The expertise IS the prompt.
What this means for you
Time spent learning is investment, not waste. AI amplifies domain expertise; it doesn't replace it. The people getting the most from AI coding tools are domain experts, not AI experts.
WHY CALIBRATE EXPECTATIONS?
Notice what Jaana DIDN'T claim: Not "10x productivity", not "replaced my team", not "production-ready", not "changed everything." She said: "useful starting point" and "toy version." This measured response is a signal of credibility.
What this means for you
Be skeptical of "X months of work in Y hours" claims without context. Ask: What was the quality? What expertise went into the prompt? What's the gap to production? "10x" claims mislead; 2-3x is often the real number.

DOMAIN-KNOWLEDGE.md - Captured Expertise

# Domain Knowledge - Agent Orchestration

## Problem Space (Months 1-3)

Multiple teams building orchestrators. No alignment on approach.
Learning: The problem is real, consensus is difficult.

The core tension: consistency vs availability during failures.

## Architecture Exploration (Months 4-6)

gRPC vs REST Analysis:
- REST: Simpler, but lacks bidirectional streaming
- gRPC: Complex setup, but enables sub-second health checks
- DECISION: gRPC wins because failover speed is critical

State Store Evaluation:
- etcd: Strong consistency, Raft consensus, k8s native
- Consul: Service mesh features, but more complexity
- DECISION: etcd for simplicity + k8s integration

## Implementation Lessons (Months 7-9)

Three failed prototypes taught us:
1. Optimistic locking insufficient for task distribution
2. Heartbeat interval < failover SLA / 3 (rule of thumb)
3. Task reassignment must be idempotent

THE KEY INVARIANT: No task lost during redistribution.

## Convergence (Months 10-12)

The architecture that survived natural selection:
- Leader-elected coordinator (via etcd lease)
- Agent heartbeats every 100ms
- Task state in etcd with version CAS
- Reassignment on 3 missed heartbeats (300ms)

5 The 3-Paragraph Technique

Jaana described the distributed agent orchestration problem in just 3 paragraphs. This structure forces conceptual clarity and gives AI the information it needs to implement effectively. 

# THE 3-PARAGRAPH TECHNIQUE FOR AI PROTOTYPING

Paragraph 1: CONTEXT
"We need a system that [does X] because [reason Y].
Currently [problem Z] exists."

Paragraph 2: REQUIREMENTS
"The system must handle [A], [B], and [C].
It needs to coordinate [entities] across [scale]."

Paragraph 3: BEHAVIOR
"When [trigger], it should [response].
The key invariant is [constraint]."

Example 1: Jaana's Distributed Agent Orchestrator

Paragraph 1: Context
"We need a distributed agent orchestration system because our AI workloads
span multiple cloud regions and require fault-tolerant coordination.
Currently we have fragmented approaches with no unified control plane."

Paragraph 2: Requirements
"The system must handle agent registration, task distribution, and health monitoring.
It needs to coordinate up to 1000 agents across 5 regions with sub-second
failover and at-least-once task delivery guarantees."

Paragraph 3: Behavior
"When an agent fails, tasks should automatically redistribute to healthy agents.
The key invariant is that no task should be lost during redistribution,
even during simultaneous multi-agent failures."

Example 2: E-commerce Recommendation Engine

Paragraph 1: Context
"We need a recommendation system that suggests products based on user behavior
because our current static lists have poor conversion rates.
Currently we show the same products to everyone, ignoring purchase history."

Paragraph 2: Requirements
"The system must handle real-time user interactions, collaborative filtering,
and inventory-aware suggestions. It needs to serve 10K recommendations/second
with less than 50ms latency and handle cold-start users."

Paragraph 3: Behavior
"When a user views a product, related items should appear within 100ms.
The key invariant is never recommending out-of-stock items or items
the user has already purchased."

Bad Example (What NOT to write)

"Build me an agent orchestrator that handles failover."
Why it's bad:
- No context (why do you need this?)
- No requirements (what scale? what guarantees?)
- No behavior (what's the expected response?)
- Claude will ask clarifying questions or guess wrong

7 Gotchas

Real problems you'll hit when applying this mental model:

1. "10x in 10 minutes" expectations
Warning sign: Disappointment when prototype has gaps
Why it happens: Headlines strip caveats. "1 year in 1 hour" sounds like magic.
Calibration: Expect toy version, not production. Prototype = validation + starting point.
2. Skipping the understanding phase
Warning sign: You can't write 3 clear paragraphs
Why it happens: Impatience. "AI will figure it out."
Calibration: If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it yet. Invest more time.
3. Using prototype as production
Warning sign: Pushing "toy" code to users
Why it happens: "It works in demo!"
Calibration: Treat prototype as starting point only. Budget 4-10x more time for production.
4. Misattributing speed to AI
Warning sign: "AI did all the work"
Why it happens: The year of learning is invisible.
Calibration: AI leveraged YOUR expertise. Without the year, no useful prompt.
5. Vague problem statements
Warning sign: Claude asks many clarifying questions
Why it happens: Unclear thinking produces unclear prompts.
Calibration: Use 3-paragraph structure. Context, Requirements, Behavior.
6. Overly detailed specifications
Warning sign: Your prompt is 10 pages long
Why it happens: Confusing specification with articulation.
Calibration: If too long, you're over-specifying. 3 paragraphs is the constraint.
7. No key invariant identified
Warning sign: Prototype misses the hard part
Why it happens: Invariant wasn't articulated explicitly.
Calibration: "The key invariant is [X]" must be in Paragraph 3.
8. Tribal hype vs anti-hype
Warning sign: "AI is hype" OR "AI replaces everything"
Why it happens: Tribal loyalty to extreme positions.
Calibration: Follow Jaana's example. Honest evaluation, give credit where due.
9. Domain expertise substitution
Warning sign: Thinking AI replaces learning
Why it happens: Misunderstanding the leverage model.
Calibration: AI amplifies expertise, doesn't replace it. More expertise = more leverage.
10. Production gap denial
Warning sign: "It works in demo" + ship to production
Why it happens: Underestimating the gap.
Calibration: Prototype != deployable. The gap is: error handling, security, observability, edge cases.

8 What's Hard

These are fundamental tensions, not bugs to fix:

Expertise Takes Time
The tension: There is no shortcut to understanding. You cannot skip the year. AI amplifies expertise; it cannot create it.
Symptoms: Wanting to use AI without domain knowledge. Frustration that prompts don't work.
Coping strategy: Accept the investment. The learning time is not waste - it's what makes AI useful later.
Articulation Is a Skill
The tension: Knowing something is not the same as explaining it. The 3-paragraph format requires practice.
Symptoms: Long rambling prompts. Claude asking many questions. Output doesn't match intent.
Coping strategy: Practice articulation separately from AI use. Write problem descriptions before opening Claude.
Toy vs Production Gap
The tension: There are many details between "working prototype" and "deployed system." This gap is often 4-10x the prototype effort.
Symptoms: Prototype demos well but fails in production. Edge cases surface. Security is missing.
Coping strategy: Budget for productionization as a separate phase. Use prototype for validation, not deployment.
Hype vs Reality
The tension: Social media amplifies extreme claims. "1 year in 1 hour" gets shared without "toy version" caveat.
Symptoms: Disappointment when reality doesn't match headlines. Tribal debates about AI capabilities.
Coping strategy: Look at caveats, not headlines. Follow practitioners like Jaana who qualify their claims.
Transferability
The tension: This worked for Jaana (expert). Will it work for you? Depends on YOUR domain expertise.
Symptoms: "I tried and it didn't work." Often means: insufficient expertise, not broken technique.
Coping strategy: Calibrate based on your domain expertise. More expertise = more leverage from this approach.
