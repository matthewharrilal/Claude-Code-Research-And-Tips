# Spatial Map: Molly Cantillon's Personal Panopticon

## Governing Spatial Metaphor

The page IS the panopticon. The reader IS the observer in the watchtower. Each section is a cell they peer into. Scrolling is rotating the gaze. The page structure should make the reader FEEL the surveillance architecture -- not illustrate it, embody it.

---

## Concept 1: The Watchtower (Persistent Observer Element)

**Content:** Applies globally -- the reader's persistent frame of reference across the entire page.
**Layout:** A sticky left-edge vertical rail (narrow, 48-60px) that persists through the entire scroll. It contains minimal markers -- small dots or abbreviated labels for each major section. As the reader scrolls, the current section indicator illuminates. This rail IS the watchtower sightline -- the reader always knows where they are relative to the whole architecture.
**What the reader sees:** A thin, quiet column on the left that tracks their position. It never demands attention but is always there. The panopticon metaphor made structural: you always have a view of the whole while examining one cell.

## Concept 2: Surveillance Asymmetry (Split Layout)

**Content:** Part I -- The Philosophy of Self-Legibility. The institutional asymmetry problem and the reversal.
**Layout:** A horizontal split. Left half: the institution's apparatus (census, cadastral maps, behavioral data). Right half: the individual's blindness (no dashboard, no alert system, no synthesis). The two halves should feel unbalanced -- the institution side dense with capability, the individual side sparse and exposed. Then at the "reversal" moment (self-legibility definition), the layout FLIPS: the individual side fills with the panopticon's tools and the asymmetry inverts.
**What the reader sees:** A visual before/after that makes the power asymmetry visceral. The left side is heavy with institutional sight. The right side is painfully empty. Then the reversal: the individual builds their own tower.

## Concept 3: The 8-Domain Constellation (Radial/Grid)

**Content:** Part II -- The 8-Domain Architecture. The domain taxonomy, design principles.
**Layout:** A 4x2 grid (work domains top row, life domains bottom row) -- NOT a vertical list. All 8 visible simultaneously. Each domain is a distinct container (a "cell") with its own border, background tint, icon, and label. The human observer sits conceptually at the center of this grid. Consider a subtle radial arrangement where the 8 domains arc around a central point, like cells around a watchtower. Each cell should have visual independence -- distinct enough to feel isolated, uniform enough to feel like parts of one system.
**What the reader sees:** 8 distinct zones, all visible at once, showing parallelism and independence. The layout screams "these run simultaneously" -- not sequentially.

## Concept 4: Isolation + Boundaries (Containerized Cells)

**Content:** Part III -- How Isolation Works. No cross-contamination, filesystem as memory.
**Layout:** The 8 domain cells from Concept 3 should have HARD BOUNDARIES. Clear borders, distinct container backgrounds, visible separation. No bleeding between cells. When illustrating isolation principles (context purity, security, blast radius), use containers-within-containers: each domain cell shows its internal directory structure as a nested element. The visual gap between cells is meaningful -- it IS the isolation.
**What the reader sees:** Distinct, sealed containers with visible walls. The whitespace between them is not decorative -- it represents the architectural isolation that prevents cross-contamination.

## Concept 5: Explicit Handoff Bridges

**Content:** Part III (handoff protocol), Part IX (correlation scenarios).
**Layout:** Between the 8-domain cells, EXPLICIT visual connectors appear only where handoffs exist. These are thin lines or arrows connecting specific cell pairs, with a small handoff indicator (a node or junction point) at the connection. The connectors are NOT always-visible -- they appear in context when discussing handoffs, or as subtle dashed lines showing potential pathways. The shared/ directory is a small central hub node that multiple connectors pass through.
**What the reader sees:** Most cell-to-cell paths are blank (isolation). A few explicit bridges show where data crosses boundaries. The bridges feel deliberate and designed, not accidental.

## Concept 6: The Correlation Layer (Meta-Observer Overlay)

**Content:** Part IX -- The Correlation Layer, Part XI -- Extensions.
**Layout:** A section that VISUALLY SPANS the full width of the domain grid. It sits above or overlays the 8 cells like a translucent observation deck. The correlation engine has read-only "sightlines" down into each domain (represented by vertical lines or arrows pointing downward into each cell). It synthesizes into a single output. This section should feel elevated -- it sees what the individual domains cannot see on their own.
**What the reader sees:** A wide, spanning element that sits above the domain grid, looking down into all 8 cells simultaneously. Symlinks visualized as downward sightlines. The synthesis output (daily brief) is the single artifact this layer produces.

## Concept 7: The Implementation Stack (Vertical Progression)

**Content:** Part IV (implementation details), Part XII (building your own).
**Layout:** A vertical progression with clear phases. Phase 1 -> Phase 2 -> Phase 3 -> Phase 4, each building on the last. Use a stepped/staircase layout or a vertical timeline with expanding detail at each phase. Code blocks within each phase should feel like literal terminal windows (dark bg, monospace, visible prompts). The checkpoints between phases are horizontal dividers with state summaries -- rest stops on the vertical climb.
**What the reader sees:** A clear ascent from setup to mastery. Each phase is a distinct vertical zone with its own character. The reader progresses upward through increasing sophistication.

## Concept 8: Warning Zones (Breaking the Pattern)

**Content:** Part VI (philosophy and warnings), Part X (anti-patterns and gotchas).
**Layout:** These sections BREAK the panopticon metaphor. They should feel visually DIFFERENT from the architectural sections. Where the architecture sections use the grid/constellation/container visual language, the warning sections use a different spatial logic: open space, fewer containers, wider margins, a sense of stepping OUTSIDE the system. Consider a full-width layout with generous whitespace, no grid, no cells -- just text in open air. The "we must continue to live outside it" section should feel like exiting the building. Possible: a subtle background shift (slightly warmer or cooler tint) to signal "you are now outside the panopticon."
**What the reader sees:** A sudden spatial relief. The tight grid of domains gives way to open space. The walls dissolve. This IS the meta-level outside the system. The reader physically experiences "stepping outside" the architecture they were just exploring.

## Page-Level Spatial Sequence

1. **ENTRY** -- Hero with the core tension: "They can see you. You cannot see yourself." Split asymmetry establishes the problem.
2. **THE REVERSAL** -- Self-legibility definition. The individual builds their own tower. The layout flips.
3. **THE ARCHITECTURE** -- 8-domain constellation appears. All 8 visible at once. This is the heart of the page spatially.
4. **ISOLATION** -- Zoom into the cells. Hard boundaries, internal structure, filesystem-as-memory.
5. **BRIDGES** -- Handoffs and connectors appear between cells. Explicit, designed, sparse.
6. **THE CORRELATION LAYER** -- Meta-observer spans the full grid from above. Sightlines down into all domains.
7. **IMPLEMENTATION** -- Vertical progression. Setup -> Automation -> Correlation -> Refinement. Terminal-window aesthetic.
8. **THE WARNING** -- Pattern breaks. Open space. No grid. "We must continue to live outside it." The reader exits the panopticon.
9. **CLOSE** -- Return to the core insight. The reader is now the observer. The page has been the panopticon all along.

---

## Self-Referential Element

The page should acknowledge its own structure at least once. A brief moment where the content references the fact that the reader has been the observer all along -- that the page layout itself mirrors the panopticon architecture it describes. This is the "self-legibility" of the page: it sees itself.

## Key Spatial Constraints

- The 8-domain grid must be visible WITHOUT scrolling (or with minimal scroll). The parallelism must hit visually, not be inferred from sequential reading.
- The persistent left rail (watchtower) must never compete with content. It is peripheral vision, not focal.
- Warning sections must feel spatially distinct from architecture sections. Different spatial logic, not just different styling.
- The correlation layer must visually span multiple domains. It cannot be a single-column section that merely describes spanning.
- Container gaps between domains are meaningful architectural elements, not padding. Size them deliberately (24-32px minimum).
