# Perceptual Audit -- Round 1, Auditor B

**Page:** Steve Yegge and Gas Town (Deep Extraction)
**Date:** 2026-03-01
**Viewports reviewed:** Wide (view-a), Medium (view-b), Narrow (view-c) -- full page + fold crops for each

---

## Cold Looks

### COLD LOOK (view-a -- wide, full page):

**Gut reaction:** This is a very long, highly structured technical document that alternates aggressively between light text-on-cream sections and dark inverted blocks, creating a stark, almost brutalist rhythm -- but it goes on so long that the rhythm becomes numbing rather than propulsive.

**Worst thing:** The sheer density of dark blocks in the lower two-thirds of the page. Once you get past the opening briefing zone, the page becomes a relentless wall of dark-on-dark code/content blocks stacked with very little breathing room between them. The alternation that starts as dramatic becomes monotonous -- like a drumbeat that never changes tempo.

**Best thing:** The opening section (the "briefing room" zone) with the Complexity Ladder diagram. The typographic hierarchy there is clear and confident -- the decorative title face, the zone label, the callout boxes with colored left edges. That opening stretch feels like it was designed; the rest feels like it was generated.

**Ship it?:** REFINE

---

### COLD LOOK (view-a -- wide, fold):

**Gut reaction:** The above-the-fold experience is genuinely inviting. Clean, spacious, confident. The dark navigation bar at top with the warm "KORTAI / DEEP EXTRACTION" branding, then generous open space leading to the zone label and the striking serif title "Steve Yegge and Gas Town." The subtitle breathes. The "You Are Here" subheading is bold and declarative. The Complexity Ladder sits in a well-framed box with a thin left accent. This is a strong entrance.

**Worst thing:** The subtitle text ("The agent factory architect building team-scale productivity for solo developers") feels slightly orphaned -- it sits between the bold title and the next bold heading without quite belonging to either. It needs either more space below or a tighter visual coupling to the title above.

**Best thing:** The Complexity Ladder callout box. It has presence without shouting. The monospace text, the blue label, the contained structure -- it reads immediately as "orientation device" and earns its prominent placement.

**Ship it?:** YES (for this fold alone)

---

### COLD LOOK (view-b -- medium, full page):

**Gut reaction:** The page adapts reasonably to the narrower viewport. Content reflows without breaking. But the dark blocks now feel even more dominant because they span a larger proportion of the visible width. The overall sensation is of scrolling through a tunnel of alternating light and dark chambers.

**Worst thing:** Same as wide: the dark block fatigue in the lower half. At medium width, the dark blocks feel even heavier because there is less lateral breathing room. The page feels like it is pressing in on you.

**Best thing:** The opening sequence still works. The title, ladder, and introductory prose all hold their proportions well at this width.

**Ship it?:** REFINE

---

### COLD LOOK (view-c -- narrow, full page):

**Gut reaction:** The narrow viewport exaggerates every issue. The dark blocks now dominate almost the entire width, creating a nearly solid dark stripe pattern when you scroll. The light sections between them feel like thin cracks of daylight. The reading experience becomes claustrophobic.

**Worst thing:** The dark code blocks at narrow width. They become crushing -- full-bleed dark rectangles stacked one after another. Whatever elegance the wide layout had in its light/dark rhythm is gone; this is just dense darkness with occasional relief.

**Best thing:** The title typography scales down gracefully. "Steve Yegge and Gas Town" in the decorative serif still reads with authority at narrow width. The hierarchy of zone label, title, subtitle remains clear.

**Ship it?:** REFINE

## Evaluation Questions

### E-07: "Pick any two adjacent sections. What changes between them?"

I am looking at the transition from the opening "Briefing Room" (Z1) into what appears to be the next major zone. The briefing room is light-background, prose-heavy, with callout boxes that have colored left-edge accents (blue for the Complexity Ladder, blue for "After This Document," amber for "Prerequisites"). It feels explanatory and inviting.

The next zone shifts into the technical content -- and the most noticeable change is the sudden arrival of large dark blocks. The background flips from warm cream to near-black. The text goes from dark-on-light to light-on-dark. The typography shifts from readable prose with a mix of body text and lists to monospace-heavy code specimens.

What actually changes: the **surface darkness** flips dramatically. The **text density** increases. The **type treatment** shifts from proportional to monospace. The **tone** goes from "let me orient you" to "here is the raw technical material."

What does NOT change (and probably should): the **rhythm**. The spacing between sections feels uniform throughout. There is no acceleration or deceleration. The transition from orientation to technical depth just... happens. There is no visual hinge, no moment where the page says "okay, now we are going deeper." The dark blocks simply start appearing and never stop.

---

### E-09: "Is there a moment where the visual treatment changes AND the content changes? Do they match?"

Yes -- the clearest one is the transition from the opening briefing zone into the first dark-background technical section. The content shifts from "here is what this document is about and who it is for" to "here is the actual Gas Town architecture." The visual treatment shifts from cream-background prose to dark-block code.

They **mostly match**. The darkness signals "we are now in the technical interior" and the content delivers on that. However, there is a mismatch in *intensity*: the visual shift is extremely dramatic (full surface inversion) while the content shift is gradual (the prose is still explanatory, just more technical). The visual treatment overshoots the content shift. It feels like walking through a door into a completely different room when really you have just moved one step further into the same conversation.

There is also a structural repetition issue: the visual treatment change happens many times (every code block inverts the surface), which dilutes the meaningfulness of the first inversion. If the dark treatment is "we are going deep," then the page is saying "we are going deep" forty times, which means it stops signaling anything.

---

### E-15: "Does this page have a dramatic peak -- a moment where the visual treatment reaches maximum intensity?"

Looking at the full-page views, the page does not have a single dramatic peak. Instead, it has a **plateau of intensity** that begins roughly one-third of the way down and sustains through the bottom. The dark blocks become so frequent and so dominant that the page achieves a kind of maximum-darkness-saturation from which it never descends.

If I had to point to a peak, it would be somewhere in the dense middle section where multiple dark blocks stack with very little light relief between them -- perhaps around the 50-60% scroll mark, where from the thumbnail view I can see an almost unbroken wall of dark rectangles. But this does not feel "earned" in the traditional sense. It does not feel like the page built up to this moment. It feels like the page simply accumulated dark blocks until they overwhelmed the composition.

The page is missing a **shape** to its intensity curve. A well-composed long page would have valleys and peaks -- moments of density followed by moments of release. This page has: gentle opening, then relentless density. It is a step function, not an arc.

The closest thing to a genuinely dramatic moment is actually the opening: the title "Steve Yegge and Gas Town" in the decorative serif face, sitting in all that open cream space, is the most visually striking single element on the page. But it is the *first* thing you see, not a peak -- it is the summit you start on before descending into the technical valley.

There also appears to be a structural break roughly two-thirds down the page where a heavy horizontal rule spans the full width. This could function as a dramatic divider -- a moment of pause and reset. But from the full-page view, it reads as just another dark element in the stream rather than a meaningful punctuation mark.

---

### E-18: "What is the single best moment on this page?"

The single best moment is the **Complexity Ladder callout box** in the opening section. It is the one element on this page that demonstrates genuine design thinking -- the integration of content, typography, and spatial treatment into something that communicates beyond what the words alone say.

The ladder uses monospace text to create a visual structure (the vertical pipe characters, the indentation, the arrow pointing to "YOU ARE LEARNING THIS") that is simultaneously content and decoration. It sits inside a contained box with a blue left accent that signals "this is a reference object, not flowing prose." The heading "THE COMPLEXITY LADDER" in small-caps blue monospace establishes the box as a named artifact.

What makes this work is that the *form mirrors the content*. A ladder drawn in text, showing levels, with your position marked -- the visual treatment IS the information. The box does not just contain text about a ladder; it IS a ladder. That is design thinking: making the container and the contained inseparable.

Everything else on the page is competent but unremarkable -- dark blocks for code, light sections for prose, accent colors for labels. The Complexity Ladder is the one place where someone thought about *what this particular content needs to look like*, not just *what category of content this is*.

## Overall Assessment

**Verdict: REFINE**

The opening is strong. The Complexity Ladder is genuinely well-designed. The typographic hierarchy at the top of the page is clear and confident. But the page does not sustain that quality as it lengthens. The dark code blocks, which are individually fine, collectively create an oppressive, monotonous reading experience -- especially on narrower viewports. The page needs valleys: moments of openness and light between the dark technical sections. It needs a shape to its intensity, not a flat plateau. The raw material is good; the composition needs sculpting.
