## PRINCIPLE EXPLANATIONS (4 Soul Choices)

**(1) Why border-radius: 0?**

Sharp corners communicate PRECISION. Scientific instruments have clean geometric edges because approximation in form would suggest approximation in function. A micrometer with rounded edges would be suspect. Lab equipment is DECISIVE — measurements are EXACT (even when error bars exist). Border-radius: 0 says "this system does not approximate."

**(2) Why 4px spacing base?**

Mathematical precision requires QUANTIZED intervals. You can't measure to "approximately 3.7px" — you measure to 4px or you don't. The spacing scale (4/8/12/16/20/24...) mirrors laboratory measurement precision: discrete values, not continuous approximations. Just as a digital caliper reads 10.00mm (not 10.003mm), spacing reads 32px (not 31px).

**(3) Why Instrument Serif for display?**

Editorial AUTHORITY in scientific writing. Research papers use serif fonts for titles to communicate "peer-reviewed, authoritative, institutional." The lab metaphor extends this: protocol titles in serif signal "verified procedure, not experimental guess." Serif = ESTABLISHED KNOWLEDGE. Sans = working instructions. Mono = DATA.

**(4) Why warm palette (40-45 hue)?**

Paradox resolution: warm palette humanizes COLD PRECISION. Lab environments use warm lighting (not harsh fluorescents) to maintain human attention during precision work. The warm cream (#FEF9F5) says "this is rigorous AND approachable." Pure white (#FFF) would be clinical/sterile. Warm palette prevents intimidation while maintaining precision.
