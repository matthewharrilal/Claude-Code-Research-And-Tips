## AUDIT QUESTION 1: Does the SKILL.md give enough concrete guidance on HOW to read content for spatial form?

### What the SKILL.md provides (lines 164-187):

The SKILL.md has a "How to Read Content for Spatial Form" section with four labeled readings:
1. **First reading (Comprehension)** -- summary, argument, emotional arc
2. **Second reading (Spatial Listening)** -- spatial words embedded in the subject matter
3. **Third reading (Structural Anatomy)** -- geometric structures the content contains
4. **Fourth reading (Hypothesis Formation)** -- form 2-3 spatial hypotheses

It also provides the "Seven Cognitive Moves That Produce Spatial Invention" (lines 178-187) as a numbered list.

### What the research provides that the SKILL.md does not:

**The research gives specific agent instructions for each reading.** The `_content-spatial-discovery.md` includes verbatim instruction text for each reading -- actual language an agent would receive. For example, the Second Reading instruction (lines 54):

> "Read the content again, this time looking for words and concepts that have inherent spatial character. List every word that implies a position, a movement, a structure, a boundary, a container, or a relationship. For each one, write what spatial form it implies. You are not inventing metaphors -- you are finding the ones already there."

The SKILL.md's Second Reading instruction (line 170-171) is:

> What spatial words are embedded in the subject matter? "Panopticon" (radial structure). "Parallel" (grid). "Gap" (spatial absence). "Widening" (expanding corridor). The agent is not inventing metaphors -- it is finding the ones already there.

**The SKILL.md gives examples of spatial words but does not tell the agent to LIST them.** The research is explicit: "List every spatial word you find. For each one, write what spatial form it implies." The SKILL.md demonstrates the move but does not instruct the agent to produce a written artifact from each reading. This is a significant gap. The written artifact is what forces vague spatial intuition into specific language.

**The research provides a complete Third Reading instruction with a structural mapping vocabulary:**
```
  - N parallel items (N > 3) = a grid
  - A progressive argument = a widening or narrowing shape
  - A duality or tension = an asymmetric split
  - A cycle or iteration = a loop or spiral
  - A hierarchy = nested containers
  - A diagnostic or choice structure = a branching tree
  - Categories with different internal logic = pattern-switching
```

The SKILL.md's Third Reading (line 172) says: "What geometric structures does the content contain? 8 parallel items = grid. Progressive argument = widening shape. Duality = asymmetric split. Cycle = loop." This captures 4 of the 7 mappings. The branching tree, nested containers, and pattern-switching mappings are absent.

**The deep analysis provides 5 distinct reading stages (E.1-E.5), not 4.** The SKILL.md stops at the fourth reading (hypothesis formation). The research's fourth and fifth reads cover fractal properties, width variation, component consistency, the page as its own system, and gaps revealed by completion. These later readings are where compound inventions like the terrain map and character-level density emerge. The SKILL.md addresses some of this implicitly in the wave descriptions, but the reading protocol itself is incomplete.

### VERDICT: PARTIAL CAPTURE. The four readings are present but under-specified. Missing: (a) explicit instruction to produce written artifacts at each stage, (b) the complete structural mapping vocabulary (3 of 7 mappings missing), (c) the 4th and 5th reading stages from the deep analysis, (d) the verbatim agent instruction language that the research demonstrated was critical for activating the right cognitive mode.

## AUDIT QUESTION 3: Did the SKILL.md capture the operational details -- the 7-step reading protocol, specific instruction phrases, and Phase 1-4 instruction set?

### The 7-Step Reading Protocol

The `_content-spatial-discovery.md` Section G provides a 7-step summary:
1. Read the content three times (comprehension, metaphor, structural anatomy)
2. Form 2-3 spatial hypotheses
3. Apply the swap test
4. Build from hypotheses, not from a template
5. For each section, ask "What is the SHAPE of what this section says?"
6. The content already contains the layout
7. 30-60 minutes before any CSS

The SKILL.md captures steps 1-3 well (lines 164-176), captures step 4 implicitly (line 440-441 in anti-patterns: "Build a standard vertical skeleton first" is rejected), mentions step 5 nowhere, captures step 6 as philosophy throughout, and captures step 7 in Wave 0 (8-12 passes of content-only reading, lines 192-201).

**Step 5 -- "For each section, ask: What is the SHAPE of what this section says?" -- is the single most actionable instruction in the entire research corpus and it does not appear in the SKILL.md.** The research emphasizes this question as a persistent per-section diagnostic. Its absence is a significant gap.

### The Phase 1-4 Instruction Set

The `_content-spatial-discovery.md` Section E provides literal code-block-formatted instructions for 4 phases:

- **Phase 1: Deep Reading** (lines 286-310) -- verbatim agent instructions for the three readings
- **Phase 2: Hypothesis Formation** (lines 314-339) -- verbatim agent instructions with good/bad hypothesis examples
- **Phase 3: The Swap Test** (lines 343-358) -- verbatim agent instructions
- **Phase 4: Building from Hypothesis** (lines 362-382) -- verbatim instructions with the critical diagnostic

**In the SKILL.md:** The four phases are present as concepts in Wave 0 and the "How to Read Content for Spatial Form" section, but the VERBATIM INSTRUCTION TEXT is absent. The research provides instruction text specifically designed for agent consumption (imperative voice, specific actions, diagnostic checks). The SKILL.md provides descriptions in expository prose aimed at a human reader.

This matters because the SKILL.md IS the agent's instruction set. When the agent reads "What spatial words are embedded in the subject matter?" (line 170), it encounters an expository question. When it reads "List every word that implies a position, a movement, a structure, a boundary, a container, or a relationship" (from the research), it encounters a direct imperative. The research found that the imperative form produces different agent behavior.

### The Good/Bad Hypothesis Examples

The research Section E Phase 2 provides explicit examples of good and bad hypotheses:

**Good:**
- "This content's 8 parallel domains demand a grid because they operate simultaneously, not sequentially."
- "This content's argument widens from one provocation to a comprehensive system, so the page should physically widen."

**Bad:**
- "This content has sections, so it should use a grid."
- "This page should be visually interesting."
- "The layout should match the content's theme."

**In the SKILL.md:** The good hypothesis examples are present (line 174 gives 4 properties of a good hypothesis). But the BAD hypothesis examples -- showing the agent what to avoid -- are absent. The contrast between good and bad is what teaches the distinction.

### VERDICT: SIGNIFICANT GAP. The operational machinery -- the imperative instructions, the per-section diagnostic question, the good/bad contrast examples, the Phase 1-4 formatted instruction blocks -- did not transfer. The SKILL.md captures the same ideas in expository prose but lacks the agent-ready instruction format that the research specifically designed.

## AUDIT QUESTION 5: Is "loss detection" adequately emphasized?

### What the research says:

The `_content-spatial-deep.md` Section E.5 identifies loss detection as the central cognitive move:

> "The specific cognitive move: **read the content and ask 'what would be lost if this were presented as prose in a single column?'**"

The section then provides a complete table mapping 7 Molly content features to their single-column losses and the spatial forms those losses name:

| Content | What is lost in a single column | What spatial form must preserve |
|---|---|---|
| 8 domains | Simultaneity | Grid |
| Institutional vs personal | Power asymmetry | Unequal columns |
| Confinement to liberation | Progressive expansion | Widening container |
| Warning section | Re-constriction | Re-narrowing container |
| File tree | Nesting/hierarchy | Tree component |
| Handoff protocol | Bridge between isolated spaces | Narrow connecting element |
| "Violent gap" quote | Emotional rupture force | Full-bleed break |

The `_content-spatial-discovery.md` lists loss detection as Cognitive Move #2 and in Section E Phase 4 provides the critical diagnostic: "If every section is a vertical column of prose, you have not found the content's spatial logic yet."

The deep analysis Section C uses the loss question as the METHOD for discovering all 15 unrealized topologies: for each content piece, it asks "what would be lost?" and the answer names the topology.

### What the SKILL.md provides:

Loss detection appears once, as Cognitive Move #2 (line 181):

> **Loss Detection:** "What would be lost in a single column?" The loss names the spatial form. If simultaneity is lost, you need a grid. If hierarchy is lost, you need asymmetry.

This is a 2-sentence entry in a list of 7 cognitive moves. It is not emphasized over the other 6 moves. It has no worked example. The critical insight -- that loss detection is the MOST POWERFUL move because it transforms an open-ended creative problem ("what spatial form should this have?") into a diagnostic problem ("what is destroyed by the default?") -- is not articulated.

The anti-patterns section (line 440) rejects "build a standard vertical skeleton first" but does not connect this rejection to loss detection. The connection is: a vertical skeleton is the thing whose losses you should be detecting. Building it first means committing to it before detecting its losses.

### What is missing:

1. The loss-detection table (7 content features mapped to losses mapped to spatial forms) -- this is the most teach-by-example material in the entire research corpus
2. The framing of loss detection as the PRIMARY cognitive move, not one of seven
3. The connection between loss detection and the swap test (both are diagnostic, not generative -- the agent does not need to INVENT, it needs to DETECT)
4. The research's insight that loss detection transforms spatial invention from a creative challenge into a pattern-recognition task -- which is what LLMs are good at

### VERDICT: SIGNIFICANTLY UNDER-EMPHASIZED. Loss detection is the research's single most important contribution to making spatial discovery teachable, and the SKILL.md treats it as one item in a list. The loss-detection table is absent. The framing as the primary (not secondary) move is absent. The connection to LLM strengths (detection over invention) is absent.

## AUDIT QUESTION 7: Does the SKILL.md teach the agent how to discover topologies in NEW content, or reference the 15 unrealized topologies?

### The 15 unrealized topologies:

Zero of the 15 are referenced in the SKILL.md. The content potential map (`_master-doc-content-potential-map.md`) provides a rich set of content-to-topology mappings across 15 different articles, each demonstrating how the same reading process applied to different content produces a different spatial form. None of this material appears in the SKILL.md.

### Teaching topology discovery for NEW content:

The deep analysis Section C.16 provides a 3-step method for discovering topologies in any content:

> **Step 1:** Read the content and ask "what is this content ABOUT in spatial terms?" Does it describe things that have positions relative to each other? Things that nest inside each other? Things that occur in sequence? Things that exist in tension?
>
> **Step 2:** Ask "what would be lost if this content were presented in a single column?" If the content describes 8 parallel agents, what is lost in a list? (The simultaneity.) If the content describes a loop, what is lost in linear sections? (The cycling.) The LOSS tells you the topology.
>
> **Step 3:** Ask "what metaphor does the content already provide?" Gas Town is a town. The trading floor is a trading floor. The panopticon is a panopticon. The content often names its own spatial form.

**In the SKILL.md:** Step 1 is roughly captured by the Second and Third readings (lines 170-173). Step 2 is partially captured by cognitive move #2 (loss detection, line 181) but without examples. Step 3 is captured by cognitive move #3 (metaphor listening, line 183).

However, the SKILL.md does not consolidate these into a single topology-discovery method. The steps are scattered across two different sections (the reading protocol and the cognitive moves) without a unifying frame. The research presents them as a focused 3-step method for answering one question: "What topology hides in this content?" The SKILL.md presents them as readings and cognitive moves without that unifying question.

### What is lost by excluding the 15 topologies:

The 15 topologies serve as EXISTENCE PROOFS. They demonstrate that the reading process, applied to wildly different content, produces wildly different spatial forms. An agent reading only the SKILL.md sees one extended example (brief references to the Molly Panopticon build) and general principles. An agent reading the content potential map sees 15 different content-to-topology mappings and develops a sense of the RANGE of possible topologies.

This matters because the agent's biggest risk is defaulting to the 2-3 topology types it has seen (corridor, grid, asymmetry) for every piece of content. The 15 unrealized topologies show that content can demand spiral loops, filtration columns, expanding stacks, paradox panels, day maps, flywheels, diagnostic trees, living systems, community constellations, compression funnels, and source trees. Without this vocabulary, the agent's imagination is constrained to what the SKILL.md's brief examples demonstrate.

The research addresses this concern directly (discovery doc line 228-233):

> Content may contain spatial forms not on this list. The list covers the 8 archetypes found across 15 extractions. A 16th extraction might reveal a 9th archetype. The agent should not limit itself to these 8 -- it should follow the content wherever the content leads.

This framing -- "here is the vocabulary we have found so far, but the vocabulary is open-ended" -- is the correct way to present the topologies without making them prescriptive. The SKILL.md could include this framing without turning the topologies into a selection menu.

### VERDICT: SIGNIFICANT GAP. The 15 unrealized topologies are completely absent. The topology-discovery method exists in scattered pieces but lacks consolidation. The agent has no existence proofs demonstrating the range of possible topologies. The risk is topology poverty -- the agent discovering the same 2-3 forms for every piece of content because its vocabulary does not include the full range.

### The Core Diagnosis

The SKILL.md captures the PHILOSOPHY of content-spatial discovery at high fidelity. The cognitive state definition (content-topology formation), the three-step mechanism (semantic saturation, spatial projection, topology persistence), the swap test, the anti-patterns, the metacognitive evaluation -- all are well-articulated.

What the SKILL.md does NOT capture is the TEACHING APPARATUS. The research contains:
- A full worked example (Molly Panopticon through all readings)
- 20 invention traces (what was being read, what was seen, why it could not have been seen earlier)
- A loss-detection table (the single most actionable teaching tool)
- 15 existence-proof topologies (demonstrating the range)
- Agent-ready instruction blocks (imperative voice, specific actions)
- Good/bad contrast examples for hypotheses
- The per-section diagnostic question ("What is the SHAPE of what this section says?")

The SKILL.md tells the agent WHAT content-spatial discovery is. The research tells the agent HOW to do it. The gap is procedural, not conceptual. The agent reading the SKILL.md understands the goal but lacks the operational scaffolding to reliably achieve it.

### The 5 Highest-Priority Additions

If the SKILL.md were to be amended, these 5 additions would close the largest gaps:

1. **The per-section diagnostic question.** Add "For each section, ask: 'What is the SHAPE of what this section says?' If the answer is 'a vertical column of prose,' the content's spatial logic has not been found yet." This is the single most actionable instruction the research produced and it is absent.

2. **The loss-detection table.** Add the 7-row table from the deep analysis (content feature, what is lost in single column, what spatial form must preserve). This teaches loss detection through concrete example rather than abstract principle, and demonstrates that loss detection is the PRIMARY cognitive move.

3. **The 8 archetype vocabulary with CSS signatures.** Not as a selection menu, but as "here are the forms we have seen hiding in content so far -- your content may contain forms not on this list." Each entry: name, 1-sentence definition, how the agent discovers it, CSS signature. The agent needs spatial vocabulary to think spatially.

4. **The hypothesis sentence template.** Add the explicit format: "This content's [specific feature] demands [specific spatial form] because [specific reason from the content]." With 2-3 good examples and 2-3 bad examples showing the contrast.

5. **One extended worked example.** Trace the Molly Panopticon through all 3 readings: 7 spatial metaphors found, 6 structural forms mapped, 3 hypotheses formed, swap test applied, spatial skeleton built. This gives the agent a pattern to match against when processing new content.
