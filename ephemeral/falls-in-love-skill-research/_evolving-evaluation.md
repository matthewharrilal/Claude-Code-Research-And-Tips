# Evolving Evaluation: How /falls-in-love Prevents Stale Assessment

**Date:** 2026-03-03
**Research basis:** PA skill (48 questions), master doc audit (13 findings), cognitive mechanism definition (10 falsifiable criteria), visual maturity gap analysis (5 dimensions), Molly/Panopticon progressive enrichment wave logs (waves 1-5)

---

## The Core Problem This Solves

The pipeline's Perceptual Audit asks the same 48 questions every cycle. By PA cycle 3, auditors are answering variations on the same themes. The evaluation becomes a compliance check wearing perceptual clothing. "Does the page have rhythm?" gets the same answer at hour 1 and hour 4, because the question does not grow with the page.

The user's insight: "Your understanding where the evaluations don't become stale, and you're just against the checklist, making sure you do everything. You're constantly re-evaluating and learning how you should be evaluating this."

This is about evaluation that deepens. Not a fixed rubric applied 50 times. The agent's understanding of what "good" means for THIS page evolves with every pass, because each pass teaches the agent something new about the content and about the page's relationship to that content.

---

## A. How Evaluation Evolves Across Passes

### The Natural Progression

Evaluation depth follows a predictable trajectory, not because it is scheduled but because it reflects how understanding accumulates. The Molly build's wave logs demonstrate this trajectory in practice.

### Phase 1: Structural Questions (Passes 1-3)

**What the agent asks:** Does the page have zones? Does the content flow through them? Is the metaphor visible? Are the bones right?

**Evidence from Wave 1:**
The Wave 1 retrospective asks entirely structural questions. "What Still Needs Work" is a list of structural absences:
- "Layout variety is LIMITED -- most zones use single-column vertical flow with occasional grids."
- "The dark zone is too long and too uniform in density."
- "Zone transitions (especially Zone 2 -> Zone 3) are still subtle."
- "The page feels competent but not EXCITING."

Every item is about whether foundational elements exist and whether they are arranged correctly. The agent is not yet asking whether the form serves the content -- it is asking whether the form exists at all. This is the correct evaluation at this stage: you cannot evaluate the marriage of form and content before the form is built.

**Characteristic questions:**
- Does each zone have a distinct visual identity?
- Does the content physically appear in the zones where it belongs?
- Is the metaphor architecturally present (not just named in CSS classes)?
- Can a reader tell where they are on the page without reading text?
- Are the zone transitions visible, or does the page feel like one undifferentiated column?

**What structural evaluation CANNOT see:** Whether the zones are the RIGHT zones. Whether the metaphor's spatial logic actually matches the content's argument structure. Whether the form is generic (could hold any content) or specific (married to this content). These questions require more time with the page.

---

### Phase 2: Relational Questions (Passes 4-6)

**What the agent asks:** Does the density rhythm work? Are the transitions between zones effective? Does the grid hierarchy communicate the content's hierarchy? How do the parts relate to each other?

**Evidence from Wave 2-3:**
By Wave 2, the agent is asking relational questions that were invisible in Wave 1:
- "Consecutive SLOW components create fatigue. Inserting a FAST component between two SLOW ones transforms the reading experience." (Wave 2)
- "The velocity model is the most directly useful finding." (Wave 2)
- "Density is rhythm, not quantity. The page should oscillate: LOW -> HIGH -> LOW -> HIGH." (Wave 2)

And by Wave 3:
- "Mode transitions need explicit markers." (Wave 3)
- "Culmination signals arrival." (Wave 3)
- "Quote variety prevents monotony." (Wave 3)

The agent is no longer asking "does Zone 4 exist?" -- it is asking "does Zone 4's relationship to Zone 3 create the right reading experience?" The evaluation has shifted from checking elements to evaluating how elements interact. This is a qualitative leap: the agent now sees the page as a SYSTEM of relationships, not a collection of parts.

**Characteristic questions:**
- Does the transition between Zone 3 (implementation) and Zone 4 (philosophy) signal the tonal shift?
- Is the density rhythm creating a reading experience or just visual noise?
- Does the bento grid hierarchy communicate that some domains are more important than others?
- When a reader finishes the dark zone, is there decompression before the synthesis?
- Do the three quote styles create variety or inconsistency?

**What relational evaluation CANNOT see:** Whether the relationships are serving THIS content specifically, or whether these same relationships would work equally well for different content. The swap test -- could you move this section to a different page without loss of meaning? -- requires an even deeper level of engagement.

---

### Phase 3: Coupling Questions (Passes 7-10)

**What the agent asks:** Does the page's spatial form ENACT the content's argument? Could this layout exist for different content? Where does the form soften -- where could it be tighter? Is the form married to the content or merely dating it?

**Evidence from Wave 4-5:**
By Wave 4, the agent's evaluation has shifted to content-form coupling:
- "Width variation creates reading interest. The margin note shifts the reader's eye from left-column flow to right-column annotation... where the eye goes matters more than what it sees." (Wave 4)
- "Bento grid hierarchy makes content legible. The featured model cell applies the same principle as the featured domain cards -- not all cells are equal because not all content is equal." (Wave 4)

And Wave 5:
- "Compound combination requires clean architecture. CD-006's 39/40 score came from deploying DD, OD, and AD mechanisms simultaneously on the same elements." (Wave 5)
- "Transition grammar creates reading rhythm. Now the reader encounters thesis -> BRIDGE -> architecture -> BRIDGE -> implementation -> CINEMATIC -> philosophy -> SUMMARY -> synthesis." (Wave 5)
- "Fractal density works at 5 simultaneous scales." (Wave 5)

The agent is now asking whether the page's specific spatial decisions derive from the content's specific meaning. The terrain map is not a generic navigation aid -- it is the page's density rhythm made visible, which matters because the content is ABOUT systematic self-observation. The bilateral asymmetry block works because the content IS about asymmetry between institutional sight and personal blindness.

**Characteristic questions:**
- Apply the swap test: if I took Zone 4's dark treatment and placed it on a different article, would the spatial decision still make sense?
- Does the corridor metaphor derive FROM the content (about institutional constraint narrowing vision) or is it applied TO the content?
- Is the bilateral asymmetry block's visual weight difference actually encoding the power imbalance the content describes?
- Where is the form generic -- where could ANY content fill these containers?
- What spatial invention does the content demand that the page has not yet discovered?

**What coupling evaluation CANNOT see:** What the page could STILL become. The difference between "no problems" and "no unrealized potential." This requires the agent to have spent enough time with the page that it can imagine versions of the page that do not yet exist.

---

### Phase 4: Potential Questions (Passes 10+)

**What the agent asks:** What would I change if I had infinite time? What is the page NOT doing that the content suggests? What spatial invention is the content hiding that I have not found yet? If this were the only page I ever built, would I be proud of it?

**Evidence from the wave logs' "What Still Could Be Explored" sections:**
By Wave 5, the agent's remaining questions are about POTENTIAL, not problems:
- "Zone 1 layout variety -- still the longest zone with the most prose. Could benefit from a different reading path or component type."
- "Color temperature variation within zones."
- "Scroll-based terrain map highlighting -- the terrain map could highlight the current zone based on scroll position."
- "More transition variety -- all bridges are text-based. A graphical divider or visual element could add variety."

These are not defects. They are unrealized possibilities. The agent can imagine versions of the page that are richer, tighter, more coupled to the content -- and each imagined version is specific to THIS page and THIS content. A flat evaluation rubric could never produce these questions because they emerge from 5 waves of intimacy with this particular page.

**Characteristic questions:**
- What would the page look like if I had 10 more hours?
- What is the content's deepest structural argument, and does the page's spatial form embody it at every scale?
- Where does the form soften -- where am I accepting "good enough" because I have been looking at it too long?
- What would someone who disagrees with my approach say? What would they change?
- If I removed all my CSS and started over with only what I now know about this content, what would I build differently?

---

### Why This Is Not a Schedule

The four phases describe how understanding naturally deepens, not a calendar to follow. A builder might ask a coupling question on pass 2 if something jumps out -- "wait, this layout could work for any content" is a valid observation at any stage. And a builder might ask a structural question on pass 10 if a late-stage change reveals that a zone boundary was in the wrong place all along.

The natural progression is structural -> relational -> coupling -> potential because:
1. You cannot evaluate relationships between parts before the parts exist.
2. You cannot evaluate content-form coupling before you understand both the content and the form well enough to see whether they match.
3. You cannot evaluate unrealized potential until you have built enough of the page that you can imagine what it COULD become.

But the progression is descriptive, not prescriptive. The agent should ask whatever questions its current understanding generates, and the skill should trust that deeper questions will emerge naturally from sustained engagement.

## B. The Anti-Stale Evaluation Principle

### Why Fixed Rubrics Go Stale

The PA's 48 questions are excellent questions. E-14 ("Is there a rhythm to this page -- a beat you can feel?") is a genuine perceptual inquiry. E-18 ("What's the single best moment on this page?") invites real judgment. The problem is not the questions themselves. The problem is that asking the SAME questions every cycle produces diminishing returns.

By PA cycle 3, the auditor has already answered "is there rhythm?" twice. The answer at cycle 3 is a variation on the cycle 2 answer, which was a variation on the cycle 1 answer. The evaluation is no longer discovering new things about the page -- it is confirming what it already knows. This is the stale evaluation trap: the rubric measures what it was designed to measure, but the page has evolved past the rubric's resolution.

### How /falls-in-love Prevents Staleness

**Mechanism 1: Self-generated questions replace fixed rubrics.**

After each wave, the agent writes "What Still Needs Work" -- a list of questions and observations specific to the current state of this page. These are not drawn from a rubric. They are generated by the agent's own experience of what it just built.

Compare:
- Fixed rubric question: "Does the page have 3+ zone transitions?" (Same answer every time after the transitions exist.)
- Self-generated question from Wave 2: "Zone 4 internal rhythm -- Still mostly uniform: heading, prose, quote, heading, dialectic, heading, quote. Need more layout variety within the dark zone." (Specific to this page's current state, and the answer changes as the page changes.)

The self-generated questions are FRESHER because they emerge from the agent's current understanding. They evolve because the agent's understanding evolves. Wave 1's "What Still Needs Work" is entirely different from Wave 5's, because the page is different and the agent's knowledge of the page is different.

**Mechanism 2: Each wave retrospective asks "What Did I Learn About the Content?"**

This is the key anti-stale mechanism. The question is not "what did I fix?" but "what did I learn?" Learning is inherently non-repeating. You cannot learn the same thing twice. Each wave teaches the agent something new about the content:

- Wave 1: "The bilateral asymmetry between institutional sight and personal blindness is THE thesis."
- Wave 2: "The velocity model is the most directly useful finding." (The agent learned that the content has velocity -- fast/slow sections that demand different spatial treatment.)
- Wave 3: "Structure visibility is educational, not decorative." (The agent learned that showing the page's own structure serves the content's theme of self-observation.)
- Wave 4: "Width variation creates reading interest... where the eye goes matters more than what it sees." (The agent learned that the content's information architecture demands asymmetric spatial treatment.)
- Wave 5: "Transition grammar creates reading rhythm." (The agent learned that the content's argument has a specific temporal structure: thesis -> architecture -> implementation -> philosophy -> synthesis.)

Each learning produces new evaluation criteria. Once the agent knows that the content's thesis is about asymmetry, it can ask: "Does the page's spatial form embody asymmetry?" That question could not have been asked before Wave 1, because the agent had not yet identified the thesis. And once the agent asks that question and finds the answer, it generates a NEW question: "Is the asymmetry just in the bilateral block, or does it pervade the page's spatial logic?"

**Mechanism 3: Periodic adversarial self-questioning.**

The agent should regularly ask:
- "What am I NOT seeing? What has become invisible to me because I have been staring at this page for hours?"
- "What would someone who disagrees with my approach say? What critique would feel uncomfortable?"
- "Am I confusing familiarity with quality? Does this section look 'right' because it IS right or because I have seen it enough times that it FEELS right?"
- "What would a fresh-eyes auditor -- someone seeing this page for the first time -- notice that I have stopped noticing?"

These questions combat the agent's own adaptation. After 5 passes, the agent has habituated to the page. Things that were jarring on pass 1 now feel normal. The adversarial self-questioning is a deliberate de-habituation: forcing the agent to see the page as if for the first time, or through hostile eyes.

**Mechanism 4: The evaluation includes taste, not just features.**

Fixed rubrics check for the PRESENCE of features: "Does the page have rhythm? Yes/No." Taste evaluates the QUALITY of features: "Does the rhythm serve the content, or does it interrupt it?" This distinction is critical because presence does not imply quality. A page can have 4 zone transitions (passing any feature-count rubric) while every transition interrupts the reading flow (failing a taste evaluation).

Taste cannot be reduced to a rubric because it requires judgment that is specific to the page. "Does this transition work?" has different answers for different pages. The transition between the implementation section and the philosophy section should feel like a tonal descent -- not because any rubric says so, but because THIS content shifts from practical to philosophical at that boundary, and the spatial form should enact that shift.

## C. The Taste Evaluation

### The Distinction Between Checklist and Taste

**Checklist evaluation:** "Does the page have 3+ zone transitions?"
- Answer: Yes (4 transitions). PASS.
- Problem: Says nothing about whether the transitions WORK.

**Taste evaluation:** "Do the zone transitions create reading rhythm, or do they interrupt it?"
- Answer: "The thesis-to-architecture bridge works -- 'The Problem Is Clear' signals the shift from argument to structure. The architecture-to-implementation bridge is weaker -- 'From the Drafting Table to the Field' feels like a chapter heading, not a tonal transition. The cinematic descent banner before the philosophy zone is the strongest transition on the page -- it feels earned because three zones of progressively denser content have built toward it. The summary strip after the dark zone works as decompression but feels slightly formulaic."
- Value: Specific, directional, and actionable. The builder knows WHICH transitions need work and WHY.

The distinction: checklists evaluate WHETHER features exist. Taste evaluates whether features achieve their PURPOSE. Taste requires having built the feature, seen it rendered, scrolled through it, and felt whether it lands.

### How to Instruct an Agent to Evaluate with Taste

The PA skill's "Experiential Pass" (Section 0) is the closest existing model: look at the page, react, lock your reaction. But the PA deploys this pass on fresh-eyes agents who have never seen the page. The /falls-in-love taste evaluation is different because it is performed by the BUILDER -- the agent who has the deepest possible context.

**Step 1: Screenshot and LOOK.**
Take a full-page screenshot at 1440px. Look at it as a whole. Do not read text. Do not analyze code. Absorb shape, color, weight, rhythm. What does the page LOOK like? Not what does it contain -- what does it look like?

This is harder than it sounds. The builder's instinct after 5 passes is to analyze: "Zone 2's domain grid needs featured cells." The taste evaluation asks the builder to suppress analysis and simply PERCEIVE. What does the page feel like as a visual object?

**Step 2: Scroll through as a reader.**
Open the page in a browser. Scroll through it at reading speed. Do not stop to inspect elements. Do not hover to check hover states. Just scroll, the way a person encountering this page for the first time would scroll.

Notice:
- Where you STOP: Something caught your eye. A strong moment. What made you stop?
- Where you SCAN: Your eye is moving fast. You are not engaged. What made you disengage?
- Where you LINGER: Something is almost working. You are trying to understand it. What is it reaching for?

**Step 3: The pride question.**
"If this were the only page I ever built, would I be proud of it?"

This question cuts through all rationalization. The agent knows the constraints it operated under, the budget it had, the passes it completed. "Would I be proud?" asks whether the result, independent of process, achieves something worth achieving. If the answer is "yes, but..." -- the "but" is the evaluation. If the answer is "no" -- the agent knows it has more work to do.

**Step 4: The specific-generic test.**
"Could this page's spatial form exist for different content?"

This is the swap test from the visual maturity analysis, applied as self-evaluation. If the agent can imagine this layout holding a different article, the form is generic. If the agent cannot imagine it -- because the corridor width IS the essay's thesis, because the bilateral asymmetry block IS the content's central argument -- the form is coupled.

### What Taste Evaluation Produces

Taste evaluation does not produce scores. It produces OBSERVATIONS. Specific, localized observations about where the page succeeds and where it falls short, grounded in the builder's accumulated understanding of the content.

Examples from the wave logs:
- "The page feels competent but not EXCITING." (Wave 1 -- a taste judgment, not a checklist result)
- "The dark zone is too long and too uniform in density." (Wave 1 -- the agent can FEEL the uniformity because it has scrolled through it)
- "Breaking the column is the strongest visual move available." (Wave 2 -- the agent has SEEN the pull quotes rendered and knows they create a visceral reading pause)
- "Culmination signals arrival." (Wave 3 -- the agent understands that the synthesis zone SHOULD feel like arrival, and noticed that it did not feel that way at 32px)

Each of these is a taste judgment that a checklist could not produce. "The page feels competent but not exciting" cannot be derived from any feature count. It can only be produced by an agent that has built the page, looked at it, and had an honest reaction.

## D. The "What Would I Change With Infinite Time" Question

### Why This Is the Most Important Evaluation Question

The standard evaluation question is: "Are there any critical issues?"

This question has a natural endpoint: "No." When there are no critical issues, the evaluation terminates. The page ships. Whether it is truly finished depends entirely on where you set the threshold for "critical."

The /falls-in-love replacement question is: "What is the most interesting thing this page could still become?"

This question has NO natural endpoint. There is always something the page could become. The corridor could narrow more gradually. The dark zone could have internal density variation that maps to the content's philosophical structure. The terrain map could respond to scroll position. The bilateral asymmetry block could have a second instance that shows the asymmetry REVERSING as the content's argument develops.

The answer drives the NEXT pass. Specifically:

**When the answer is a spatial invention waiting to happen:**
"I wish the domain grid felt more like looking through 8 doors." -- This is a content-topology insight. The 8 domains are not just categories; they are TERRITORIES the subject has explored. A "doors" metaphor would imply passage -- clicking a domain takes you somewhere. This insight could drive a pass that adds depth effects, perspective transforms, or interactive exploration to the domain grid.

**When the answer is a refinement target:**
"The dark zone is dramatic but the transition out of it is weak." -- This is a relational insight. The dark zone's drama is not landing because the recovery does not give the reader time to surface. This insight drove the Wave 5 addition of the summary strip -- a decompression layer between the dark zone and the synthesis zone.

**When the answer is about coupling:**
"The zone transitions are consistent but they all use the same format -- prose bridges. Could a transition between zones be SPATIAL instead of verbal?" -- This is a coupling insight. The content shifts from practical to philosophical; the spatial form should enact that shift, not just describe it. A cinematic descent banner (added in Wave 2) is a spatial transition. But the other three bridges are verbal. Could one of them be a visual element -- a gradient, a density shift, a typographic event -- that enacts the transition without words?

### The Endpoint

The endpoint is NOT "no more answers." The endpoint is when the answer becomes so fine-grained that the changes would be invisible to a reader.

The progression:
- Pass 1: "The page needs more layout variety." (Coarse -- visible to any observer)
- Pass 3: "Zone 4 needs internal rhythm variation." (Medium -- visible to an attentive reader)
- Pass 5: "Character-level letter-spacing could vary by element type." (Fine -- visible only at close inspection)
- Pass 7: "The letter-spacing on benchmark quotes should be -0.015em, not -0.01em." (Invisible -- a reader would not notice the difference)

When the agent's "infinite time" answer is at the invisible grain, the page is done. Not because there are no problems -- there are always refinements. But because the remaining refinements would not change the reader's experience. This is genuine satisfaction: not "no problems" but "no unrealized potential that would make a visible difference."

This is the distinction from the master doc audit's Finding 5.1: perfectionism stops when flaws are absent; devotion stops when unrealized potential is absent. The "infinite time" question operationalizes this distinction. A perfectionist asks "what is wrong?" and stops when nothing is wrong. A devoted builder asks "what could this become?" and stops when the answer is invisible.

## E. Evaluation as Learning

### The Self-Feeding Loop

Each evaluation teaches the agent something about the content. This is not a side effect -- it is the mechanism by which evaluation deepens.

**The loop:**
```
BUILD (apply current content understanding to spatial form)
  -> EVALUATE (look at the result; notice where the form serves the content
               and where it does not)
    -> LEARN (the gaps between form and content reveal content structure
              the agent had not previously noticed)
      -> BUILD BETTER (the new content understanding produces more
                       coupled spatial decisions)
        -> EVALUATE (the closer coupling reveals finer content structure)
          -> LEARN MORE (...)
```

**Evidence from the wave logs:**

Wave 1 BUILD: The agent builds 5 zones based on the cartographic survey metaphor.
Wave 1 EVALUATE: "The bilateral asymmetry between institutional sight and personal blindness is THE thesis."
Wave 1 LEARN: The agent did not KNOW the thesis before building. Building the zones -- and looking at them -- revealed that the content's deepest argument is about asymmetry. The act of placing content into spatial zones made the content's structure visible to the builder.

Wave 2 BUILD: The agent adds density rhythm (pull quotes, sidebar notes, cinematic descent).
Wave 2 EVALUATE: "Density is rhythm, not quantity."
Wave 2 LEARN: The agent discovered through building that the content has VELOCITY -- some sections are fast (callouts, principles) and some are slow (code blocks, philosophical arguments). This velocity was always in the content, but it became visible only when the agent tried to create reading rhythm and noticed where the content resisted.

Wave 3 BUILD: The agent adds bento hierarchy to the domain grid and zone annotations.
Wave 3 EVALUATE: "Structure visibility is educational, not decorative."
Wave 3 LEARN: The zone annotations ("Zone 1 of 5 -- The Datum Point") are not just navigation. They serve the content's THEME: the article is about self-observation, and the zone annotations are the page observing its own structure. Making the page's architecture visible to the reader is not decoration -- it is the page enacting the content's argument about legibility.

This is the deepest insight: the evaluation does not just check the page. It teaches the agent about the content. The agent who built Zone 4 as a dark zone knew the content shifted to philosophy at that point. But the agent who EVALUATED Zone 4 and noticed "the dark zone is too long and too uniform" learned something new: the philosophical section has more internal structure than the current spatial form captures. There are objections, trade-offs, failure modes -- subsections that deserve distinct spatial treatment within the dark zone. The evaluation revealed content structure that the builder had flattened.

### Why This Loop Prevents Stale Evaluation

A fixed rubric asks the same questions every time because it does not learn. The 48 PA questions are the same at cycle 1 and cycle 10 because the rubric's understanding of the page does not change.

The self-feeding loop produces DIFFERENT evaluation questions every time because the agent's content understanding changes. After Wave 1, the agent can ask: "Does the bilateral asymmetry block communicate the thesis?" After Wave 3, the agent can ask: "Do the zone annotations serve the content's theme of self-observation?" After Wave 5, the agent can ask: "Does the 5-scale fractal density system embody the content's argument about systematic self-legibility at every scale of life?"

Each question is more specific than the last. Each requires deeper content understanding. And each can only be asked by an agent that has spent the preceding waves building, evaluating, and learning. A fresh-eyes auditor could never ask "Does the 5-scale fractal density system embody the content's argument?" because that question requires having built the fractal density system and understanding what it is FOR.

This is the anti-stale mechanism in its purest form: evaluation that grows because the evaluator grows. Not a fixed rubric applied to a changing page, but a changing understanding applied to a changing page, where each change in understanding produces new questions that the previous understanding could not have generated.

### The Implication for /falls-in-love Skill Design

The skill should NOT provide a fixed list of evaluation questions. It should provide:

1. **A progression model** (structural -> relational -> coupling -> potential) that tells the agent what KIND of questions to ask at each depth level, without specifying the questions themselves.

2. **A self-generation protocol** that requires the agent to produce its own "What Still Needs Work" and "What I Learned About the Content" after each wave. These self-generated outputs become the evaluation criteria for the next wave.

3. **Adversarial prompts** that periodically force the agent to question its own habituation: "What am I not seeing?" and "What would a disagreeing critic say?"

4. **The taste protocol** (screenshot -> scroll -> pride question -> swap test) that anchors evaluation in perceptual experience rather than analytical measurement.

5. **The "infinite time" question** as the terminal evaluation: "What would I change if I had infinite time?" When the answer is invisible-grain, the page is done.

6. **The learning question** as the loop driver: "What did this evaluation teach me about the content that I did not know before?" This question guarantees that the next wave's building will be informed by deeper content understanding, which will produce a page that reveals more about the content when evaluated, which will produce deeper learning, which will produce better building.

The result is an evaluation system that cannot go stale because its questions emerge from the agent's evolving relationship with the content. The page is done not when a rubric is satisfied but when the agent's understanding has reached the depth where further refinement would be invisible. That is the genuine satisfaction described in the build philosophy: not compliance, not the absence of flaws, but the exhaustion of visible potential.

## Summary: Fixed Evaluation vs Evolving Evaluation

| Property | Fixed (PA) | Evolving (/falls-in-love) |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Questions | 48 predetermined | Self-generated each wave |
| Evaluator | Fresh-eyes auditor (no context) | The builder (maximum context) |
| What it measures | Presence of features | Quality of content-form coupling |
| What it produces | Scores and verdicts | Observations and learning |
| How it terminates | "No critical issues" | "No visible unrealized potential" |
| Staleness risk | High (same questions every cycle) | Low (questions evolve with understanding) |
| Content learning | None (evaluator is context-starved) | Primary output (evaluation teaches content) |
| Taste | Absent (binary feature checks) | Central (pride question, scroll experience) |
| Depth trajectory | Flat (same depth every cycle) | Deepening (structural -> relational -> coupling -> potential) |

Both have value. The PA's fresh-eyes context-free evaluation catches things the builder has habituated to. The /falls-in-love evolving evaluation catches things that only deep familiarity can see. The ideal system uses BOTH: the builder evaluates with taste after every wave (evolving evaluation), and a fresh-eyes auditor evaluates periodically (fixed evaluation) to catch the builder's blind spots.

But the builder's evaluation is PRIMARY. The builder is the one who knows whether the corridor width IS the thesis or merely holds it. The builder is the one who can imagine what the page COULD become. The builder is the one whose evaluation deepens the content understanding that produces the next wave's spatial invention. The fresh-eyes audit is a CORRECTION on the builder's evaluation, not a replacement for it. The builder drives; the auditor course-corrects.
