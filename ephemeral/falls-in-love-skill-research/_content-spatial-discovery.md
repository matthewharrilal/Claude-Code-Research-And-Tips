# Content-Spatial Discovery: How the Builder Sees the Shape Hiding in the Content

**Date:** 2026-03-03
**Purpose:** Define the precise cognitive protocol by which a builder agent discovers spatial forms embedded in content -- the core creative act that the /falls-in-love skill must enable
**Sources:** Content potential map (15 extractions, 8 spatial archetypes), cognitive mechanism definition (10 falsifiable criteria, 7 enabling conditions), visual maturity gap analysis (5 dimensions), Molly Panopticon extraction, Wave 1 progressive enrichment log

---

## The Core Thesis

The pipeline asks: "What mechanisms should be deployed?" The right question is: "What spatial form does this content demand?"

These are not two ways of asking the same thing. They produce categorically different output. The first treats content as material to be packaged. The second treats content as a structure to be revealed. The difference is the difference between pouring concrete into a mold and carving marble along the grain.

The content potential map proves this across 15 extractions. Every piece of content has a spatial topology hiding inside it -- a shape that emerges from what the content IS ABOUT, not from what layout techniques the builder knows. Gas Town is a town map. Ralph is a spiral loop. The Panopticon is a widening corridor. The Failure Modes catalog is a branching diagnostic tree. No two topologies are the same. The pipeline produces pages that are all the same. The content demands pages that are all different.

This document defines how an agent discovers that shape.

---

## A. THE CONTENT-READING PROTOCOL

The agent must read the content three times before touching any CSS. Not three skims. Three structurally different readings, each with a different lens, each producing a different kind of output. The purpose of three readings is not thoroughness -- it is cognitive mode-shifting. Each reading activates a different kind of attention.

### First Reading: COMPREHENSION

**Question:** What is this about? What is the argument? What is the emotional arc?

The first reading is conventional. The agent reads to understand. It identifies the thesis, the structure, the key claims, the evidence, the emotional trajectory. It produces a summary that any competent reader could produce.

For the Molly Panopticon extraction, the first reading yields: This is about a woman who built a personal surveillance infrastructure -- 8 Claude instances monitoring 8 life domains -- to achieve the same clarity about herself that institutions achieve about their subjects. The argument moves from philosophical provocation (institutional asymmetry) through architectural specification (8 domains, isolation, handoffs) through implementation (code, automations) to philosophical resolution (Goodhart warning, the value proposition). The emotional arc is: outrage at asymmetry, empowerment through construction, caution about excess.

This is necessary but insufficient. A competent summarizer produces this. A builder needs it as foundation, not destination.

**Agent instruction:** "Read the content fully. Write a 3-5 sentence summary of the argument. Identify the emotional arc in 1 sentence. Name the 3-5 major structural sections."

### Second Reading: METAPHOR EXTRACTION

**Question:** What spatial metaphors are EMBEDDED in the subject matter itself?

This is the reading that matters most. The agent reads not for meaning but for SHAPE. It looks for words and concepts that have inherent spatial character -- words that describe positions, movements, structures, relationships, containers, boundaries, progressions.

For the Molly Panopticon extraction, the second reading yields:
- **Panopticon** = a watchtower at the center of a circular prison. The observer sees all cells. The observed cannot see the observer. This is a radial spatial structure with a power asymmetry between center and periphery.
- **Self-legibility** = making the invisible visible. Reversing the direction of sight. This implies a spatial INVERSION -- what was external becomes internal, what was looking outward now looks inward.
- **8 parallel domains** = 8 independent entities operating simultaneously. This is a grid, a constellation, a set of rooms. The number 8 and the word "parallel" are spatial claims.
- **Isolation** = boundaries between domains. Walls. Containers. No cross-contamination. The architecture is about SEPARATION, which is fundamentally spatial.
- **The "violent gap"** = the distance between how you thought you were living and how you are living. A gap is a spatial concept -- two things that should be adjacent but are not.
- **"Seeing around corners"** = expanded field of vision. Wider. More panoramic. The surveillance metaphor implies widening sight.
- **Progressive liberation** = from institutional blindness to personal sight. A movement from constrained to expansive. A corridor that widens.

These are not metaphors the builder invents. They are metaphors the CONTENT contains. The word "panopticon" is in the title. The word "parallel" is in the architecture description. The word "gap" is in the key quote. The agent's job is to notice them, not to create them.

**Agent instruction:** "Read the content again, this time looking for words and concepts that have inherent spatial character. List every word that implies a position, a movement, a structure, a boundary, a container, or a relationship. For each one, write what spatial form it implies. You are not inventing metaphors -- you are finding the ones already there."

### Third Reading: STRUCTURAL ANATOMY

**Question:** What structures does the content CONTAIN? Not what it says about structures -- what structures it IS.

The third reading is analytical. The agent examines the content's own architecture -- its sections, its lists, its comparisons, its categories -- and identifies the geometric forms those structures embody.

For the Molly Panopticon extraction, the third reading yields:
- **8 parallel domains** = a grid. Not a list -- 8 items that operate simultaneously and independently. A grid shows simultaneity; a list implies sequence. The domains ARE a grid.
- **4 work domains + 4 life domains** = a duality within the grid. An asymmetric split. The professional and personal halves of a life, given equal structural weight but different optimization functions.
- **Institutional sight vs personal blindness** = a bilateral asymmetry. Two columns of unequal power. The institution's column should be heavier, more dominant. The personal column should be lighter, more vulnerable. The visual weight difference IS the argument.
- **Progressive argument from provocation to architecture to implementation to philosophy** = a widening corridor. The argument starts narrow (one philosophical claim) and widens (to 8 domains, to code, to automations, to daily workflows). The page should physically widen as the argument expands.
- **Isolation principle** = contained elements. Each domain is a box. The box has walls. Things do not leak between boxes. The spatial form is cellular -- grid cells with visible borders and no shared space.
- **Handoff protocol** = connections between isolated containers. Thin lines between boxes. The handoffs are narrow, explicit, controlled -- unlike the domains themselves, which are rich and complex. Narrow connections between wide containers.

The key insight of the third reading: spatial forms are not APPLIED to content. They EMERGE from content. The 8 parallel domains do not "suggest" a grid -- they ARE a grid. The bilateral asymmetry does not "suggest" unequal columns -- it IS unequal columns. The agent's job is to see the grid that is already there.

**Agent instruction:** "Read the content a third time, this time examining its structure. How many parallel categories does it contain? Are there dualities, progressions, hierarchies, cycles, or branching paths? For each structural element, name its geometric form. 8 parallel items = a grid. A progressive argument = a widening or narrowing shape. A duality = an asymmetric split. A cycle = a loop or spiral. A hierarchy = nested containers. Write these down. These are not design ideas -- they are structural facts about the content."

### The Three Readings Summarized

| Reading | Lens | Question | Output |
|---------|------|----------|--------|
| First | Comprehension | What is this about? | Summary, argument, emotional arc |
| Second | Metaphor | What spatial words are embedded in the subject? | List of spatial metaphors with implied forms |
| Third | Anatomy | What geometric structures does the content contain? | List of structural elements mapped to shapes |

The three readings take 20-40 minutes. They are not optional. A builder who skips them will default to vertical stacking because it has no spatial hypothesis to override the default. The readings produce the raw material from which the spatial hypothesis is formed.

## B. THE SPATIAL HYPOTHESIS

After three readings, the agent has accumulated two lists: embedded spatial metaphors (from Reading 2) and structural geometries (from Reading 3). From these, the agent forms 2-3 spatial hypotheses. A spatial hypothesis is a structural claim about what the content IS -- not a decoration idea, not a layout preference, but a statement about the content's inherent geometry.

### What a Spatial Hypothesis Looks Like

For the Molly Panopticon extraction, three spatial hypotheses emerge:

**Hypothesis 1: "This content's argument has a widening arc -- the page should physically widen."**

The argument moves from a narrow philosophical provocation (one observation about institutional asymmetry) to a wide architectural specification (8 domains, code, automations, daily workflows). The content EXPANDS. The page should expand with it. A corridor that starts narrow (perhaps 600px for the opening provocation) and widens to full width (960px for the 8-domain grid) physically enacts the argument's trajectory from single insight to comprehensive system.

Evidence from the content: "seeing around corners," "being in ten places at once," "attention span of a thousand clones" -- all phrases that describe EXPANSION of capacity.

**Hypothesis 2: "This content has 8 parallel domains that operate independently -- the page should show them simultaneously."**

The 8 domains are not sequential. They run in parallel. They are independent but collectively form the system. A grid is the only spatial form that honors this simultaneity. A list destroys it -- reading domains 1 through 8 implies they are sequential, that domain 1 comes before domain 2. They do not. They coexist. A grid shows coexistence.

Evidence from the content: "8 parallel Claude instances," "parallel execution," "you can run all 8 simultaneously without conflicts."

**Hypothesis 3: "This content has a surveillance/freedom duality -- the page should use asymmetry to encode power imbalance."**

The entire argument rests on an asymmetry: institutions can see you, but you cannot see yourself. This asymmetry is not a side point -- it is THE thesis. The spatial form should make this asymmetry visible. Unequal column widths (a heavy institutional column vs a lighter personal column), unequal visual weight (dark/bold vs light/muted), unequal border treatment (a thick institutional border vs a thin personal border).

Evidence from the content: "The asymmetry is total: They can see you. You cannot see yourself the same way they do."

### Properties of a Good Spatial Hypothesis

1. **It is about the content, not about the layout.** "The page should use a 2-column grid" is a layout idea. "The content's institutional/personal duality demands unequal columns" is a spatial hypothesis. The difference: the hypothesis explains WHY the layout must take a specific form.

2. **It is falsifiable.** You can test it. If the content had 3 domains instead of 8, the grid hypothesis would change. If the argument narrowed instead of widened, the corridor hypothesis would reverse. The hypothesis is coupled to THIS content, not to layout preferences.

3. **It passes the swap test.** Take the hypothesis and apply it to different content. "This content's argument widens, so the page should widen" -- would this work for an article about data compression? No, that article's argument NARROWS. The hypothesis is content-specific.

4. **It drives CSS decisions.** Every `grid-template-columns` value, every `max-width`, every `padding` can be evaluated against the hypothesis. "Does this serve the widening corridor?" If yes, keep it. If no, reconsider. The hypothesis is a decision criterion, not a decoration theme.

### How Many Hypotheses?

Two to three. One is too few -- a single hypothesis becomes a gimmick, applied everywhere. Four or more is too many -- they fragment the page into disconnected spatial ideas. The sweet spot is 2-3 hypotheses that operate at different scales:

- One hypothesis about the PAGE-LEVEL shape (widening corridor, compression funnel, spiral loop)
- One hypothesis about the SECTION-LEVEL structure (8-domain grid, hub-and-spoke, branching tree)
- Optionally, one hypothesis about a COMPONENT-LEVEL invention (self-referential observation module, pattern echo grid, accumulating sidebar)

The page-level hypothesis provides the dominant spatial logic. The section-level hypothesis provides internal variety. The component-level hypothesis provides the moment of spatial invention that makes this page unlike any other.

**Agent instruction:** "From your metaphor list and structure list, form 2-3 spatial hypotheses. Each hypothesis must: (a) reference specific content, (b) name a specific spatial form, (c) explain why the content demands that form. Write them as sentences: 'This content's [feature] demands [spatial form] because [reason].' These hypotheses will drive every CSS decision you make."

## C. THE SPATIAL VOCABULARY: 8 Discovered Forms

The content potential map reveals 8 spatial archetypes across 15 extractions. These are not mechanisms to be deployed. They are FORMS that hide in content, waiting to be discovered. The builder does not choose from this list. The builder discovers one of these forms (or a form not on this list) by reading the content deeply enough to see it.

### 1. Width as Argument

**What it is:** The page's width changes to enact the content's arc. Not decorative width variation -- the width IS the argument. A corridor that widens as the argument expands. A funnel that narrows as complexity compresses. An hourglass that narrows at the thesis and widens at evidence.

**Content that contains this form:** Arguments with progressive expansion or compression. Molly's Panopticon (narrow provocation widening to comprehensive system). Jaana Dogan (year of expertise compressed into 3 paragraphs, then expanded by AI into a prototype). Vincent Quigley (95% garbage narrowing to workable output through refinement).

**How the agent discovers it:** The second reading finds words like "widening," "expanding," "compressing," "narrowing," "funneling." The third reading finds a progressive argument that moves from narrow (one claim) to wide (many implications) or vice versa. The agent asks: "Does this content's argument get wider or narrower as it progresses?" If the answer is not "neither," width-as-argument is present.

**CSS signature:** `max-width` values that change between sections. The page does not have a uniform `max-width: 960px` -- different sections have different widths, and the width progression maps to the argument's progression. Early sections at 600-700px. Middle sections at 800-900px. Full expansion at 960px. Or the reverse for compression.

**The swap test:** Take a widening corridor and apply it to content about data compression. The width progression contradicts the argument. The form fails the swap. It is content-derived.

### 2. Grid as Taxonomy

**What it is:** Content that contains parallel categories becomes a grid. Not a grid for visual variety -- a grid because the content IS a taxonomy of simultaneous, independent items.

**Content that contains this form:** Any content with N parallel items where N > 3 and the items operate independently. Molly's 8 domains. Gas Town's 8 roles. Dexter Horthy's 12 factors. Frontier Voices' 6 innovators.

**How the agent discovers it:** The third reading identifies lists of parallel items. The agent asks: "Are these items sequential (reading them in order matters) or simultaneous (they coexist and order is arbitrary)?" If simultaneous, a grid IS the content's spatial form. A list implies sequence. A grid implies simultaneity.

**CSS signature:** `display: grid` with `grid-template-columns` matching the number of items or a logical subdivision. Named `grid-template-areas` using content vocabulary (e.g., `"nox metrics email growth" "trades health writing personal"` for the 8 domains). Non-uniform cell sizes encoding relative importance.

**The swap test:** Take an 8-cell domain grid and apply it to content about a linear 8-step process. The grid destroys the sequential logic. The form fails the swap. It is content-derived.

### 3. Asymmetry as Power

**What it is:** Content with power dynamics, dualities, or imbalances gets unequal column ratios. The visual weight difference IS the power difference.

**Content that contains this form:** Any content structured around tension between two unequal forces. Molly's institutional-vs-personal asymmetry. Boris Cherny's vanilla-vs-power paradox. Jaana's hype-vs-reality tension. Vincent's garbage-vs-value ratio.

**How the agent discovers it:** The second reading finds words like "asymmetry," "imbalance," "tension," "versus," "paradox," "contrast." The third reading identifies two-sided structures where the sides are NOT equal. The agent asks: "Is there a power relationship between these two sides?" If yes, the columns should be unequal. The dominant side gets more space (3fr vs 1fr, 7:3 ratio). Or the subordinate side gets more space to subvert the power dynamic (institutional opacity in a narrow column, personal clarity in a wide column -- the layout REVERSING the power relationship as the content does).

**CSS signature:** `grid-template-columns: 3fr 1fr` or `7fr 3fr` or similar asymmetric ratios. Different border weights on different sides. Different background treatments encoding different levels of authority. The specific ratio maps to the specific power relationship described in the content.

**The swap test:** Take a 3:1 asymmetric split encoding institutional dominance and apply it to content about two equally-weighted alternatives. The asymmetry implies a hierarchy that does not exist. The form fails the swap.

### 4. Loop as Cycle

**What it is:** Content about iterative processes gets circular or returning layout. The page itself loops -- elements return, patterns repeat at higher levels, the reading path curves back.

**Content that contains this form:** Any content about repetitive processes, feedback loops, or iterative refinement. Ralph's prompt-execute-check-repeat cycle. Dan Shipper's compound loop. Vincent's three-attempt refinement. Kaushik's maintenance-pruning loop.

**How the agent discovers it:** The second reading finds words like "loop," "cycle," "iteration," "repeat," "return," "again," "spiral." The third reading finds process descriptions that end where they began (but at a higher level). The agent asks: "Does this process return to its starting point?" If yes, a circular or spiral spatial form is hiding in the content.

**CSS signature:** Elements that visually return to earlier positions. A persistent circular diagram. Numbered steps arranged in a ring rather than a column. Content that accumulates with each "revolution" -- a sidebar or footer that grows denser as you scroll, enacting the accumulation that the process describes.

**The swap test:** Take a spiral loop layout and apply it to content about a linear, one-directional process (e.g., a manufacturing pipeline). The circular form contradicts the linear logic. The form fails the swap.

### 5. Compression as Bottleneck

**What it is:** Content about convergence, distillation, or focus gets physically narrower at the point of maximum compression. The layout enacts the intellectual bottleneck.

**Content that contains this form:** Any content where many inputs converge to a single output, or broad scope narrows to specific focus. Jaana's year of expertise compressed into 3 paragraphs. Dan Shipper's meta-prompting (human idea through 5 layers of abstraction to implementation). The Failure Modes catalog's diagnostic funnel (9 categories narrowing to one root cause).

**How the agent discovers it:** The second reading finds words like "compress," "distill," "converge," "focus," "funnel," "bottleneck," "narrow." The third reading finds many-to-one or one-to-many structures. The agent asks: "Does this content move from broad to narrow, or from narrow to broad?" If yes, the page width at the compression point should be physically narrower than the surrounding sections.

**CSS signature:** `max-width` that decreases at the point of maximum intellectual compression, then potentially widens again. A section that is visually tight -- narrow, high-density, small type -- surrounded by sections that are spacious.

### 6. Self-Reference as Meta

**What it is:** Content about observation, monitoring, or self-awareness gets a page that observes ITSELF. The page is not just about self-surveillance -- it performs self-surveillance.

**Content that contains this form:** Any content about metacognition, monitoring, self-awareness, or systems that observe themselves. Molly's panopticon (the page should observe its own reading patterns). Claude-Flow's self-adapting swarm. OD-006's fractal self-similarity (the page documenting its own fractal structure).

**How the agent discovers it:** The second reading finds words like "observe," "monitor," "self-," "meta-," "awareness," "reflection," "mirror." The third reading finds content whose subject matter could apply to the page itself. The agent asks: "Could this content describe what this page is doing?" If yes, a self-referential component is hiding in the content. The page should contain an element that references its own structure -- a scroll witness that tracks reading progress, an annotation layer that comments on the page's own typography, a component that shows the page's own density rhythm.

**CSS signature:** `position: sticky` or `position: fixed` elements that persist across scroll positions and update based on page state. Pseudo-elements that annotate the page's own grid structure. Components with `aria-label` values that reference the page's own organizational logic. Named CSS custom properties that reference the page's own spatial vocabulary.

### 7. Negative Space as Absence

**What it is:** Content about gaps, missing information, blindness, or absence gets literal gaps in the layout. The whitespace IS the argument.

**Content that contains this form:** Any content about what is missing, unseen, or absent. Molly's "violent gap" between self-perception and reality. The institutional blindness that pre-modern states had. The context window's "dumb zone" (40-60% degradation). Vincent's "95% garbage" -- the waste is the dominant spatial feature, not the value.

**How the agent discovers it:** The second reading finds words like "gap," "blind," "missing," "absent," "invisible," "void," "empty." The third reading finds content structured around what is NOT there rather than what is. The agent asks: "Is the absence or the presence more important to this argument?" If absence, the layout should make absence visible through deliberate, meaningful whitespace -- not decorative spacing, but whitespace that represents something specific that is missing.

**CSS signature:** Sections with unusually generous `padding` or `margin` that create visible voids. Grid cells that are deliberately empty (`grid-column: 2 / 3` with the cell containing nothing). Content positioned to leave conspicuous gaps that the reader notices and feels.

### 8. Pattern-Switching as Enactment

**What it is:** Content about different patterns, modes, or approaches uses different layouts for each -- the page's spatial structure changes as the content's subject changes. Each section does not just describe a pattern; it performs that pattern.

**Content that contains this form:** Any content that catalogs or compares distinct approaches, patterns, or modes. AD-006's 5 axis patterns (the Z-pattern section uses Z-layout, the spiral section uses spiral-layout). Gas Town's 8 roles (each role has a different spatial relationship to the center). Eric Buess's stack (each layer adds visible complexity). The Failure Modes catalog's 9 categories (each category has a different failure topology).

**How the agent discovers it:** The third reading identifies multiple distinct categories, approaches, or patterns described in sequence. The agent asks: "Are these categories structurally identical, or does each one have a different internal logic?" If each has different internal logic, each section should have a different spatial skeleton. The page becomes a gallery of spatial forms, each one demonstrating the pattern it describes.

**CSS signature:** Multiple distinct `grid-template-areas` definitions within the same page. Different `grid-template-columns` formulas for different sections. The layout diversity ratio (distinct inner layouts / total sections) approaches 1.0 rather than the pipeline's typical 0.12.

### The Vocabulary Is Discoverable, Not Prescriptive

These 8 forms are not a menu. The agent does not select "Form #3: Asymmetry as Power" from a dropdown. The agent reads the content, finds the word "asymmetry," follows it to the concept of institutional-vs-personal power imbalance, and discovers that unequal columns are the spatial form this content demands. The vocabulary exists to name what the agent discovers, not to constrain what it can discover.

Content may contain spatial forms not on this list. The list covers the 8 archetypes found across 15 extractions. A 16th extraction might reveal a 9th archetype. The agent should not limit itself to these 8 -- it should follow the content wherever the content leads.

## D. THE SWAP TEST

The definitive test for content-form coupling. Every spatial decision the agent makes should be evaluated against this test.

### The Test

Take a section's layout and mentally swap it to a different page about different content. Ask: "Does this layout survive the swap?"

- If the layout survives -- it works equally well for different content -- the layout is generic. It is packaging, not argument. Push deeper.
- If the swap destroys meaning -- the layout makes no sense for different content -- the layout is content-derived. It is argument, not packaging. Keep it.

### Examples

**FAILS the swap test (generic layout):**
A 960px centered column with left-aligned prose and a `1fr 1fr` comparison grid. This works for Gas Town content AND Panopticon content AND any other content. It is a generic container. Evidence from the visual maturity analysis: "Take any section from P1 (Yegge) and swap it into P2 (Molly). Does the section break? No."

**PASSES the swap test (content-derived layout):**
AD-006's spiral section with golden ratio grid (`1fr 1fr 1.618fr`), named areas that create an L-shaped reading path, and color-coded confidence borders (blue=validated, green=established, amber=exploratory, purple=speculative). Swap this to the Molly Panopticon page. It makes no sense. The golden ratio narrows toward depth because the content is about deepening epistemic layers. Molly's content is not about epistemic layering. The layout is married to its content.

**PASSES the swap test (content-derived layout):**
The Molly build's widening corridor where `max-width` increases from the opening provocation to the full 8-domain architecture. Swap this to content about data compression (Jaana Dogan). The widening contradicts the compression. The layout is married to its content.

### How to Apply the Test

The agent should apply the swap test at three levels:

1. **Page level:** "Could this page's overall shape (widening corridor, spiral, hub-spoke) work for a different article?" If yes, the page-level spatial hypothesis is too generic.

2. **Section level:** "Could this section's grid/layout work in a different section of this same page, or on a different page entirely?" If yes, the section-level spatial treatment is generic.

3. **Component level:** "Could this component (callout, card, quote treatment) appear on any page about any topic?" If yes, the component is generic. Pipeline builds fail this test at every level: every callout, every card, every blockquote is interchangeable across all pages.

**Agent instruction:** "For every spatial decision you make, ask: 'Could this layout work for different content?' If the answer is yes, you have not yet found the content's spatial logic. Push deeper. What about THIS content makes THIS layout necessary? When the answer to 'could this work elsewhere?' is 'no, because [specific content reason],' you have found a content-derived form."

## E. HOW TO INSTRUCT AN AGENT TO DO THIS

This is the hardest part. You cannot say "invent a spatial form." Invention is not a command. But you CAN create conditions under which spatial discovery happens. The cognitive mechanism research identifies the preconditions: hypothesis-driven framing, direct content access, absence of procedural interruption, and sufficient time. The instruction set below creates those conditions.

### The Problem with Direct Commands

"Be creative" does not produce creativity. "Invent a spatial form" does not produce spatial invention. "Fall in love with the content" does not produce the falls-in-love state. These commands describe the OUTPUT, not the PROCESS. An agent that receives "be creative" has no actionable procedure to follow. It defaults to its training distribution, which produces competent but generic output.

The instruction set must describe the PROCESS -- the sequence of cognitive operations that, when followed faithfully, produce spatial discovery as a natural byproduct. The agent does not need to be told to invent. It needs to be led through a process that makes invention inevitable.

### The Instruction Set (Ordered)

**Phase 1: Deep Reading (20-40 minutes)**

```
BEFORE YOU WRITE ANY CSS, read the content three times.

FIRST READING: Read for comprehension. What is this article about? What is
the argument? What is the emotional arc? Write a 3-5 sentence summary.

SECOND READING: Read for spatial metaphors. Go through the content looking
for words that have inherent spatial character -- words about positions,
movements, structures, boundaries, containers, progressions, gaps, layers,
cycles, or relationships. List every spatial word you find. For each one,
write what spatial form it implies. You are not inventing metaphors -- you
are finding the ones the content already contains.

THIRD READING: Read for structural anatomy. Examine the content's own
architecture. How many parallel categories does it contain? Are there
dualities? Progressions? Hierarchies? Cycles? Branching paths? For each
structural element, name its geometric form:
  - N parallel items (N > 3) = a grid
  - A progressive argument = a widening or narrowing shape
  - A duality or tension = an asymmetric split
  - A cycle or iteration = a loop or spiral
  - A hierarchy = nested containers
  - A diagnostic or choice structure = a branching tree
  - Categories with different internal logic = pattern-switching
```

**Phase 2: Hypothesis Formation (10-15 minutes)**

```
From your spatial metaphor list and structural anatomy, form 2-3 SPATIAL
HYPOTHESES. A spatial hypothesis is a structural claim about what the
content IS -- not a layout preference.

Write each hypothesis as a sentence:
  "This content's [specific feature] demands [specific spatial form]
   because [specific reason from the content]."

Examples of GOOD hypotheses:
  - "This content's 8 parallel domains demand a grid because they operate
     simultaneously, not sequentially."
  - "This content's argument widens from one provocation to a comprehensive
     system, so the page should physically widen."
  - "This content's institutional/personal power imbalance demands unequal
     columns because the visual weight difference IS the thesis."

Examples of BAD hypotheses (too generic):
  - "This content has sections, so it should use a grid."
  - "This page should be visually interesting."
  - "The layout should match the content's theme."

Your hypotheses drive EVERY CSS decision. Before writing any CSS property,
ask: "Does this serve one of my spatial hypotheses?" If the answer is no,
reconsider.
```

**Phase 3: The Swap Test (5 minutes)**

```
Before you build, test your hypotheses. For each one, ask:

  "Could this spatial form work for a completely different article about
   a different subject?"

If the answer is YES, your hypothesis is too generic. Push deeper. What
about THIS content -- specifically, uniquely, THIS content -- makes this
spatial form necessary?

If the answer is NO -- if swapping this layout to different content would
destroy its meaning -- your hypothesis is content-derived. Proceed.

The goal: by the time you write CSS, you should be able to explain for
EVERY spatial decision why it could NOT work for different content. If you
cannot explain this, you are decorating, not designing.
```

**Phase 4: Building from Hypothesis (the remaining time)**

```
Now build. But build FROM your spatial hypotheses, not from a template.

DO NOT start with a standard vertical skeleton and add spatial ideas into
it. Your spatial hypothesis IS the skeleton. If the hypothesis says
"widening corridor," the page's foundational structure is a container whose
width changes. If the hypothesis says "8-domain grid," the page's core
structure includes that grid as a primary architectural element, not as a
module inserted into a vertical column.

For each section, ask:
  "What is the SHAPE of what this section says?"

If every section is a vertical column of prose, you have not found the
content's spatial logic yet. Go back to your hypotheses. At least 3-4
sections should have spatial skeletons that differ from each other, because
the content's internal sections have different structures.

The content already contains the layout. Your job is to see it.
```

### Key Phrases That Unlock the State

Certain phrases in the instruction set are more important than they appear. They shift the agent's cognitive frame from "deployer" to "discoverer":

- **"You are not inventing metaphors -- you are finding the ones the content already contains."** This reframes the task from creative generation (intimidating, high variance) to pattern recognition (familiar, reliable). The agent is good at finding patterns. Tell it the patterns are already there.

- **"What is the SHAPE of what this section says?"** This question forces spatial thinking about content. The agent cannot answer it without forming a content-to-space mapping.

- **"If every section is a vertical column, you have not found the content's spatial logic yet."** This is a DIAGNOSTIC, not a command. It tells the agent how to recognize when the process has not yet worked, without prescribing what the result should look like.

- **"The content already contains the layout. Your job is to see it."** This is the core reframe. The layout is not something the builder ADDS. It is something the content CONTAINS. The builder's role shifts from constructor to excavator.

- **"Could this layout work for different content?"** The swap test as a persistent self-check. Every time the agent reaches for a symmetric `1fr 1fr` grid, it must ask whether that grid is content-derived or generic.

### What NOT to Say

- **"Be creative."** Means nothing. Produces nothing.
- **"Use interesting layouts."** "Interesting" is not a spatial concept.
- **"Make it visually rich."** Directs attention toward decoration, not structure.
- **"Deploy mechanisms."** Frames the builder as an implementer, not a discoverer.
- **"Sample 2-4 mechanisms from the catalog."** This is the most destructive instruction identified in the research. It replaces discovery with selection from a menu.
- **"Build a standard vertical skeleton first."** This is the single most destructive constraint identified in the cognitive mechanism analysis. It commits the builder to a spatial framework BEFORE it has formed a content topology. The topology can only exist as a module inserted into slots, never as the page's foundational spatial logic.

### The Timing Matters

The three readings must happen BEFORE any CSS. Not interleaved with CSS. Not "read a section, build a section." The entire content must be read three times before the first `display: grid` is written. The reason: the content topology is a WHOLE-CONTENT phenomenon. The widening corridor hypothesis only emerges from seeing the full arc of the argument. The 8-domain grid hypothesis only emerges from seeing all 8 domains in context. Section-by-section reading produces section-by-section layouts. Whole-content reading produces whole-content spatial logic.

The Wave 1 retrospective from the Molly Panopticon progressive enrichment build confirms this. The Wave 1 builder read the content and identified: "The bilateral asymmetry between institutional sight and personal blindness is THE thesis. Everything else flows from it." This is a whole-content spatial insight. It led to the asymmetry block in Zone 1 where "the visual weight difference between the two columns communicates the power imbalance before reading." But the same Wave 1 builder also noted: "Layout variety is LIMITED -- most zones use single-column vertical flow with occasional grids." The builder found ONE spatial hypothesis (asymmetry) but did not yet find the others (widening corridor, 8-domain grid, self-referential observation). This is why multiple readings are necessary -- and why the builder's reflection that it needs to "explore in Wave 2" research-informed layout strategies shows that discovery is iterative and deepens over time.

## F. THE EVIDENCE: WHAT HAPPENS WHEN THIS PROCESS WORKS

### The Molly Panopticon Build (Content-Spatial Discovery in Action)

The content potential map documents the Molly Panopticon as the one extraction whose spatial potential has been realized. The build achieved: 3,779 lines, 53/55 maturity score, 18 DOM skeletons, 4 INEVITABLE coupling grades. The spatial forms discovered:

1. **Widening corridor** -- page width physically enacts the argument's expansion from narrow provocation to comprehensive system. This was discovered through the content's own progression, not applied from a template.

2. **Asymmetry panels** -- the institutional/personal split rendered as unequal columns with different visual weight. The builder saw "asymmetry" in the content and made it spatial.

3. **8-door domain grid** -- the 8 parallel domains rendered as a grid because they ARE a grid. Not because grids are visually interesting, but because simultaneity demands a grid.

4. **Self-observation module** -- a component where the page observes itself. Discovered because the content IS about self-observation. The form emerged from the subject matter.

5. **Self-referential narration** -- the page narrating its own structure. Discovered because the content's subject (building a surveillance apparatus) applies to what the page itself is doing (building a reading apparatus).

Each of these forms passes the swap test. The widening corridor does not work for content about compression. The asymmetry panels do not work for content about equal alternatives. The self-observation module does not work for content that is not about observation.

### What the Pipeline Produces Without This Process

The same content, built by the pipeline, produces: dark header, then vertical prose sections with `max-width: 65ch`, one `1fr 1fr` grid for domain cards, and alternating background colors via `:nth-child(even)`. The visual maturity analysis summarizes: "A page about a panopticon -- about observation and being observed from all angles simultaneously -- is laid out as a single linear column. No spatial tension between observer and observed. No layered perspectives. No sense of being watched or watching."

The pipeline builder did not read the content three times. It did not form spatial hypotheses. It did not ask "what is the shape of what this section says?" It processed the content as material to be placed in zones. The zones are generic. The content is specific. The gap between them is the gap between packaging and argument.

## G. SUMMARY: THE DISCOVERY PROCESS IN ONE PAGE

1. **Read the content three times.** First for comprehension. Second for spatial metaphors embedded in the subject matter. Third for structural anatomy -- the geometric forms hiding in the content's own architecture.

2. **Form 2-3 spatial hypotheses.** Each one is a structural claim: "This content's [feature] demands [spatial form] because [reason]." The hypotheses are about the content, not about layout preferences.

3. **Apply the swap test.** For each hypothesis, ask: "Could this spatial form work for different content?" If yes, the hypothesis is too generic. Push deeper until the answer is no.

4. **Build from the hypotheses, not from a template.** The spatial hypotheses ARE the skeleton. Do not build a vertical column and insert spatial ideas into it. Build the spatial ideas and flow content through them.

5. **For each section, ask: "What is the SHAPE of what this section says?"** If the answer is "a vertical column of prose," the content's spatial logic has not been found yet.

6. **The content already contains the layout.** The agent's job is not to invent. It is to see. The 8 parallel domains do not "suggest" a grid. They ARE a grid. The widening argument does not "suggest" a corridor. It IS a corridor. The agent excavates; it does not construct.

The entire process takes 30-60 minutes before any CSS is written. This investment is non-negotiable. A builder who skips the readings will default to vertical stacking because it has no spatial hypothesis to override the default. The readings produce the hypothesis. The hypothesis produces the spatial form. The spatial form produces the page that could not exist for any other content.
