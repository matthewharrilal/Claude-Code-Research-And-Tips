## 1. What the Case Studies Are

The case studies are Layer 5 of a 6-layer design ontology. They sit between the mechanism catalog (Layer 3, abstract techniques) and the guidelines (Layer 6, semantic rules). Each documents what ONE metaphor produced for ONE specific content -- not "how to build a geological page" but "how tension analysis of API confidence documentation led to geological strata as an organizing metaphor, and what that looked like."

There are 9 text case studies across 3 families:
- **DD (Density Dimension):** Islands (32/40), Layers (35/40), Fractal (36/40)
- **OD (Organizational Dimension):** Conversational (37/40), Confidence (34/40), Creative (37/40)
- **CD (Combination Dimension):** Reasoning-Inside-Code (38/40), Multi-Axis-Transition (38/40), Pilot-Migration (39/40)

The scores tell a progression story: complexity increases from DD to CD, and within each family the highest-scored exploration demonstrates the most sophisticated compositional thinking.

## 2. The Tension Between Calibration and Copying

Every case study opens with the same anti-gravity warning: "THIS IS NOT A TEMPLATE." The entire framework fights a specific failure mode: a builder reads the geological case study, sees 4px borders on "bedrock" layers, and copies those exact values for their own hierarchical content. That is pattern-matching, not composing.

But builders DO need to know what "designed" looks like. A builder who has never seen a thoughtfully composed page will produce flat output regardless of how many mechanisms they know. The case studies solve this through a structural separation:

**What transfers (mechanisms):** Border-weight gradient, spacing compression, zone backgrounds, 2-zone component DNA, dense/sparse alternation, width variation, velocity interleaving, transition grammar, fractal self-similarity, progressive disclosure.

**What does NOT transfer (metaphors):** Geological vocabulary, island counts, specific padding values, Q&A pair formatting, 5-layer structure, stratum labels, section-to-axis mappings, dark-to-light color progressions.

The distinction is surgical: mechanisms are CSS TECHNIQUES that work across any metaphor. Metaphor-specific decisions are the PARTICULAR APPLICATION of those techniques to THIS content with THIS spatial metaphor. A builder learns the former (vocabulary) and invents the latter (sentence).

## 3. Cross-Case-Study Patterns: What Makes Pages Feel "Designed"

After reading all 9 case studies, 5 compositional patterns appear in every successful page regardless of metaphor, content type, or axis pattern. These are the hallmarks of DESIGNED output:

### Pattern A: The Metaphor Collapse

Every high-scoring case study describes a "collapse moment" -- the instant when multiple independent tensions resolve into a single spatial idea. This is the most reliable signal that composition is happening rather than layout.

DD-004 (Geological): Three separate tensions (expert vs novice, sparse vs dense, foundational vs experimental) collapse into ONE spatial metaphor (vertical depth). The depth gradient simultaneously encodes expertise level, information density, and structural importance.

OD-001 (Conversational): Two separate problems (organize as Q&A AND create density rhythm) collapse into ONE structural choice. Q&A format doesn't just CREATE pulse rhythm -- it IS pulse rhythm.

CD-006 (Pilot Migration): Three problems (demonstrate without prescribing, prove all patterns work, maintain pedagogical flow) collapse into ONE meta-strategy. The tutorial IS the artifact being built.

**What this tells a builder:** A designed page has ONE core spatial idea that resolves MULTIPLE tensions simultaneously. If your page has separate solutions for separate problems, you haven't found the collapse yet. Keep searching until multiple concerns converge into a single structural gesture.

### Pattern B: Mechanisms Reinforce Each Other

In every high-scoring case study, mechanisms don't operate independently -- they encode the SAME semantic through DIFFERENT channels. This is multi-coherence: the experience of multiple CSS properties saying the same thing at the same zone boundary.

DD-004 (Geological): At every stratum boundary, FOUR things change simultaneously:
- Background color shifts (lighter to darker)
- Spacing compresses (80px to 16px)
- Border weight changes (4px to 1px)
- Typography scale drops (36px to 12px)

All four encode ONE idea: "you are going deeper." No single channel carries the meaning alone. The perceptual experience is richer because the channels are redundant, not additive.

OD-004 (Confidence): Border weight (4px certain, 1px uncertain), spacing (sparse certain, dense uncertain), and background progression all encode the SAME semantic -- epistemic confidence. The inverse density-confidence correlation means certain knowledge needs few words (sparse) while uncertain knowledge needs many (dense).

**What this tells a builder:** At every zone boundary on your page, ask: "Do at least 3 CSS channels change here?" If only the background changes, the boundary is sub-perceptual. If background + spacing + borders + typography all shift, the reader FEELS the transition without needing to analyze it.

### Pattern C: The Metaphor Dictates, Not Decorates

In every successful case study, the metaphor GENERATES structural decisions rather than being applied ON TOP of structural decisions. The distinction is between a metaphor that determines how many sections exist, what order they appear in, how spacing works -- versus a metaphor used as decoration (geological class names on a standard layout).

DD-003 (Islands): The island metaphor DICTATED 80px ocean padding (islands must FLOAT in visible water), horizontal offset rhythm (islands don't stack in perfect columns), and archipelago grouping (some islands cluster). Remove the metaphor and you'd design differently. The metaphor is load-bearing.

DD-006 (Fractal): The fractal metaphor DICTATED self-similarity at ALL 4 scales (not just 2). If the page showed rhythm at page level but not component level, it wouldn't be fractal -- it would be sectioned. The metaphor imposed a constraint that made the page harder to build but more coherent.

CD-001 (Reasoning-Inside-Code): The velocity interleaving insight DICTATED that Heavy-Heavy component stacking was prohibited. The metaphor (velocity management) generated a concrete rule that shaped every component placement decision.

**What this tells a builder:** Your metaphor should be making decisions FOR you. If you could remove the metaphor labels and the layout would be identical to any other page, the metaphor is decorative, not structural. A structural metaphor makes the page look different than it would without the metaphor.

### Pattern D: Content Drives Structure, Not Vice Versa

Every case study begins with Phase 1-2 tension analysis of SPECIFIC content. The metaphor emerges FROM the content, not from a library of available metaphors.

DD-003 started with: "Database documentation has 4 independent topics. Readers need one specific type, not all four."
OD-001 started with: "React Server Components content has implicit Q&A structure. Section headings are questions."
OD-004 started with: "API best practices have variable epistemic status. Some claims are industry-standard, others are speculative."

None of these started with: "Let's use the geological pattern" or "Islands would look nice here." The content's tensions -- its internal oppositions, its audience needs, its structural properties -- generated the metaphor.

**What this tells a builder:** Before you design anything, interrogate your content. What are its natural tensions? Where does it fight itself? Where does the reader's need oppose the content's structure? The answers to these questions ARE the design.

### Pattern E: Scores Correlate with Compositional Depth, Not Decoration

The case study scores reveal a clear pattern:

| Score | Case Study | What Made It Score High |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| 32/40 | DD-003 Islands | Moderate tension, proven pattern for reference docs |
| 34/40 | OD-004 Confidence | Strong epistemic encoding, inverse density-confidence |
| 35/40 | DD-004 Layers | Triple encoding (depth + time + density), widest research base |
| 36/40 | DD-006 Fractal | Meta-content alignment, same rhythm at 4 scales |
| 37/40 | OD-001 Conversational | Q&A IS pulse rhythm (format = mechanism) |
| 37/40 | OD-006 Creative | 6 organizational modes proving a thesis through experience |
| 38/40 | CD-001 Reasoning | Velocity interleaving discovery, F-to-Bento transition |
| 38/40 | CD-005 Transition | 3 axes with full transition grammar |

The progression is not about visual polish -- it is about compositional DEPTH. Higher scores come from:
- More tensions resolved by a single metaphor (DD-004: 3 tensions)
- More CSS channels encoding the same semantic at each boundary (multi-coherence)
- Metaphor generating structure rather than decorating it
- Self-referential alignment (the page IS what it teaches)
- More mechanisms combined while maintaining coherence

**What this tells a builder:** Quality is not about adding more visual effects. It is about deepening the relationship between content, metaphor, and mechanism. A page with 3 mechanisms that ALL encode the same semantic through different channels will score higher than a page with 10 mechanisms applied independently.

## 4. The Mechanism Vocabulary That Recurs

Across all 9 case studies, certain mechanisms appear repeatedly. These are the CORE vocabulary of compositional design -- the techniques that prove useful regardless of content type or metaphor:

### Tier 1: Universal (appears in 7+ case studies)

**2-Zone Component DNA (Mechanism #2):** Sparse label zone (uppercase, monospace, small) + dense content zone. Used in every case study without exception. This is the atomic unit of composition -- if your components don't have this internal rhythm, they feel flat.

**Border-Weight Gradient (Mechanism #1):** 4px/3px/2px/1px encoding hierarchy, importance, confidence, or structural role. Appears in 7 of 9 case studies. The single most transferable technique -- border weight is perceptually powerful and semantically flexible.

**Zone Background Differentiation (Mechanism #7):** Subtle background shifts between major sections. Appears in 7 of 9 case studies. Creates "you have entered a new territory" feeling without heavy borders.

### Tier 2: Frequent (appears in 4-6 case studies)

**Spacing Compression (Mechanism #4):** Padding decreases along a meaningful axis (depth, density, importance). Used in DD-003 (islands), DD-004 (geological), DD-006 (fractal), OD-004 (confidence), CD-001 (CRESCENDO).

**Dense/Sparse Alternation (Mechanism #5):** Content alternates between compressed and breathing zones. Used in DD-006 (fractal), OD-001 (conversational), OD-006 (creative), CD-001 (CRESCENDO).

**Velocity Interleaving (CD-F-001):** Never stack same-velocity components. Heavy-Fast-Heavy instead of Heavy-Heavy. Used in CD-001, CD-005, CD-006.

**Transition Grammar (AD-F-025):** SMOOTH (shared component bridges compatible patterns), BRIDGE (prose paragraph resets between incompatible patterns), BREATHING (full separator for isolated patterns). Used in CD-001, CD-005, CD-006.

### Tier 3: Situational (appears in 1-3 case studies)

**Width Variation:** Narrow entry points, wide immersive content (OD-001 conversational).
**Horizontal Offset Rhythm:** Left/right/center alignment for organic scatter (DD-003 islands).
**Fractal Annotation Layer:** Persistent sidebar showing active structural scale (OD-006 creative).
**Pattern Echo Visual Index:** CSS-only miniatures previewing content patterns (OD-006 creative).
**Culmination Spread:** Amplified synthesis section that signals ARRIVAL (OD-006 creative).
**Mode-Transition Breathing:** Cognitive reset between organizational mode switches (OD-006 creative).
**Inverse Density-Confidence Correlation:** Certain = sparse, uncertain = dense (OD-004 confidence).

## 5. What Distinguishes Each Exploration Family

### DD (Density): How information is packed in space

DD case studies answer: "What visual density pattern serves this content?" They explore the relationship between content's information density and the page's spatial density.

- **DD-003 Islands:** Content as archipelago -- dense clusters in calm ocean. ISOLATION is the spatial principle.
- **DD-004 Layers:** Content as geological strata -- depth encodes audience, density, and stability simultaneously. DEPTH is the spatial principle.
- **DD-006 Fractal:** Content as self-similar rhythm -- same dense/sparse pattern at page, section, component, and character levels. RECURSION is the spatial principle.

The DD explorations teach builders that information density is not just "how much stuff" -- it is a semantic channel. Dense zones signal importance, certainty, or technical depth. Sparse zones signal entry points, breathing room, or conceptual overview. The contrast between dense and sparse is what creates rhythm.

### OD (Organizational): How information is arranged for reading

OD case studies answer: "What organizational structure serves this content's reading patterns?" They explore how content's inherent structure generates its reading experience.

- **OD-001 Conversational:** Questions create navigation landmarks; answers deliver depth. Width variation (narrow Q, wide A) IS the organizational principle.
- **OD-004 Confidence:** Epistemic certainty stratifies content into discrete layers. Border-weight gradient encodes how much you should trust each claim.
- **OD-006 Creative:** Meta-documentation -- the page proves its thesis by BEING the proof. Six organizational modes coexist on one page, each generating its own density pattern automatically.

The OD explorations teach builders that organization and density are not separate concerns. OD-006 proved the core thesis: "Organization IS Density." When you choose a conversational Q&A structure, PULSE rhythm is already there. When you choose geological confidence stratification, spacing compression is already there. The organizational choice generates the density pattern.

### CD (Combination): How multiple patterns coexist

CD case studies answer: "What happens when you combine density + organization + axis patterns on one page?" They explore the interactions between previously-isolated techniques.

- **CD-001 Reasoning-Inside-Code:** Discovered velocity interleaving -- the insight that component READING SPEED matters more than component TYPE for sequencing decisions.
- **CD-005 Multi-Axis Transition:** Proved that 3 axis patterns (Z, F, Bento) can coexist with SMOOTH and BRIDGE transition grammar. No cognitive whiplash when boundaries are handled correctly.
- **CD-006 Pilot Migration:** The crown jewel (39/40). Five axis patterns, all 11 component types, meta-tutorial structure. Proved the full compositional pipeline works.

The CD explorations teach builders that combination is not just "use many things." It is about managing the INTERACTIONS between techniques. Velocity interleaving prevents heavy-component stacking. Transition grammar prevents cognitive whiplash at axis boundaries. Sequential deployment (one axis per section) prevents attention topology conflict.

## 6. How to Provide Calibration Without Templates

The fundamental tension: builders need to FEEL what quality looks like, but seeing specific implementations creates template-copying behavior. The case studies solve this through several structural choices that a handoff document should preserve:

### Strategy 1: Expose Process, Not Product

Every case study spends more space on HOW the metaphor was found than on WHAT the metaphor produced. The tension table, the rejected candidates, the collapse moment, the metaphor-dictated structure -- these are the value. A builder reading the process learns to SEARCH for metaphors. A builder reading only the product learns to COPY implementations.

**For the handoff:** Describe the SEARCH process (tension analysis, metaphor candidates, collapse moment) rather than the final layout. The builder should internalize the method of arriving at composition, not the composition itself.

### Strategy 2: Separate Transferable from Specific

Every case study has Section 5 with explicit checkmarks and crosses. Reusable mechanisms get checkmarks. Metaphor-specific decisions get crosses. This is aggressive disambiguation -- the case study literally tells you "this transfers" and "this does not."

**For the handoff:** When citing any example from the case studies, always frame it in terms of the MECHANISM being demonstrated, not the SPECIFIC VALUES used. "Border-weight gradient encodes hierarchy" (transferable) vs "4px bedrock borders for high confidence" (not transferable).

### Strategy 3: Require Divergence

Section 6 of every case study forces builders to compare their metaphor against the case study. If convergence is detected, strong evidence of independent derivation is required. If evidence is weak, regeneration is mandatory. This is the anti-copying enforcement mechanism.

**For the handoff:** Frame case study examples as PROOF that something is possible, not as PATTERNS to follow. "A page CAN have zone boundaries where 4 CSS channels shift simultaneously" is calibration. "Use backgrounds + borders + spacing + typography at each section break" is a template.

### Strategy 4: The Jazz Framing

The README uses Charlie Parker as the defining analogy. Parker learned 200+ standards and transcribed 100+ solos. He did not play those standards verbatim in performance. He EXTRACTED harmonic patterns, melodic shapes, rhythmic devices and applied them to NEW material.

**For the handoff:** The case studies are the Real Book. The builder's page is their solo. Knowing the standards deeply (studying the mechanisms) enables improvisation (original composition). But playing the standards verbatim (copying layouts) is not jazz -- it is karaoke.

## 7. Quality Signals for Builder Calibration

From the case studies, here are the QUALITATIVE signals that distinguish designed from flat output. These are not rules but perceptions -- what a reader FEELS:

### A Designed Page Feels Like...

**Arriving somewhere.** Each zone has a distinct sense of place -- you know when you've entered a new section because the visual environment changes (background, spacing, border weight). Not dramatically, but definitively.

**Breathing.** Dense zones are followed by sparse zones. The compression is not constant -- it alternates, creating rhythm. The reader's cognitive load oscillates between engagement and rest.

**Coherent at every zoom level.** Whether you see the whole page (zones alternate), a single section (paragraphs alternate), a single component (label + body), or a single code block (code + comments) -- the same organizational principle is recognizable.

**Intentional.** Every visual choice has a reason rooted in the content. Border weight is not decorative -- it encodes confidence. Spacing compression is not space-saving -- it encodes depth. Background shift is not variety -- it encodes territory change.

**Surprising but not arbitrary.** The metaphor makes the familiar strange. API documentation organized as geological strata SURPRISES (you haven't seen that before) but is not ARBITRARY (depth genuinely encodes confidence). The surprise comes from the aptness of the mapping, not from randomness.

### A Flat Page Feels Like...

**Uniform.** Same spacing, same backgrounds, same border weights throughout. No sense of zone or territory.

**Disconnected.** Components exist independently -- callouts next to code blocks next to prose with no rhythmic relationship. Each element is well-formed but the page has no compositional logic.

**Decorative.** Metaphor labels exist ("bedrock", "strata") but the visual treatment is identical across zones. The metaphor is announced but not enacted.

**Template-derived.** You've seen this layout before. Standard blog post structure. Heading, paragraph, code block, callout, repeat. The content could be swapped for any other content without changing the layout.

## 9. What Builders Should Take Away

### Learn the Vocabulary (Mechanisms)
A builder who knows border-weight gradient, spacing compression, 2-zone DNA, dense/sparse alternation, zone backgrounds, velocity interleaving, and transition grammar has the TOOLS to compose. These are the chords and scales.

### Learn the Process (Tension Analysis)
A builder who knows how to interrogate content for tensions, search for metaphor candidates, reject poor fits, and arrive at a collapse moment has the METHOD to compose. This is the ear training.

### Trust the Content
The content tells you what it needs. Content with discrete independent topics wants isolation (islands). Content with depth gradients wants stratification (geological). Content with Q&A structure wants pulse rhythm (conversational). The design emerges from listening to the content, not from selecting from a pattern catalog.

### Build at Boundaries
The quality of a page lives at its ZONE BOUNDARIES -- the places where one territory ends and another begins. At each boundary, ask: "What changes here?" If the answer is "only the heading," the boundary is flat. If the answer is "background shifts, spacing changes, border weight transitions, typography adjusts," the boundary is designed.

### One Idea, Many Channels
The single most reliable indicator of quality across all 9 case studies: ONE semantic idea expressed through MULTIPLE CSS channels simultaneously. Not "add more effects" but "make all effects say the same thing." Geological depth = darker backgrounds AND compressed spacing AND heavier borders AND larger typography. Four channels, one meaning.

## 10. Anti-Copying Safeguards

This digest intentionally avoids:
- Specific CSS values from any case study (no "80px padding" or "4px border")
- Complete metaphor descriptions that could be pattern-matched (no "geological with 5 strata named Atmosphere/Surface/Topsoil/Subsoil/Bedrock")
- Layout blueprints (no "Section 1 = Z-Pattern, Section 2 = F-Pattern, Section 3 = Bento")
- Copy-paste component recipes (no HTML/CSS snippets from case studies)

What this digest DOES provide:
- The QUALITIES of designed output (what it feels like, not what it looks like)
- The PROCESS patterns that recur across all successful compositions (collapse, multi-coherence, content-driven structure)
- The MECHANISM VOCABULARY at appropriate abstraction level (what the tools are, not how they were used in specific pages)
- The CALIBRATION anchors (which case studies represent ceiling quality and why)
- The QUESTION a builder should ask at each design decision ("does my metaphor dictate structure, or decorate it?")

The goal is conviction without prescription: a builder who reads this should KNOW what quality feels like, UNDERSTAND what makes it emerge, and be EQUIPPED to create it -- without having a specific layout to copy.
