# Phase Transition Reliability: Can a Single-Session Builder "Fall in Love"?

**Date:** 2026-03-03
**Analyst:** Transition Reliability Analyst (Task #17)
**Purpose:** Assess the probability that the phase transition (Phase 2 to Phase 3) occurs reliably in the proposed $91-101 architecture, where the builder has zero accumulated context from the exploration era.
**Sources:** Sustained Engagement Map (08), Synthesis (11), Invention Archaeology (07), Cognitive Mechanism Definition (02), Visual Maturity Decomposition (09)

## 1. Accumulated Context Decomposition

The exploration-era builders operated with a massive accumulated context stack. The proposed architecture gives the builder essentially none of it. Here is what that stack contained, decomposed into its atomic components, and an honest assessment of whether curated research can substitute.

### Component A: CSS Vocabulary Breadth (337 research findings)

**What it was:** 337 findings across R1-R5, covering density mechanisms, organizational patterns, axis patterns, combination strategies, and scale techniques. This gave builders a vast catalog of spatial ideas they could PROJECT onto content. When AD-006's builder read about progressive depth, the vocabulary "golden ratio narrowing" was already available as a spatial possibility because R4 had documented it.

**Can curated research substitute?** PARTIALLY. The curated structural researcher (Phase 2, Agent 2A) can compile implementation-mapped spatial techniques. But the curation is a selection -- maybe 20-40 techniques from the 337. The builder's hypothesis space is narrower. More critically, the builder has not EXPERIENCED these techniques. Reading "golden ratio columns create proportional emphasis" is different from having SEEN a golden ratio grid in a completed page. The vocabulary is abstract rather than experiential.

**What is lost:** The combinatorial range. With 337 findings, the builder can form connections between distant spatial ideas (a density finding + an axis finding = a new compositional insight). With 20-40 curated techniques, the combinatorial surface is smaller by an order of magnitude. The probability of a NOVEL combination -- the kind that produces Type C compositional inventions -- drops proportionally.

### Component B: Design Pattern Familiarity (24 prior explorations)

**What it was:** 24 completed HTML pages spanning density, organization, axis, and combination dimensions. Each page was a WORKED EXAMPLE of content-driven spatial design. The CD-006 builder knew what a hub-spoke implementation LOOKED like because AD-006 had built one. This is not knowledge about hub-spoke as a concept -- it is familiarity with hub-spoke as a spatial artifact rendered in CSS.

**Can curated research substitute?** POORLY. The curated research can describe techniques and provide CSS snippets. But snippets are not pages. A snippet of `grid-template-columns: 1fr 1.618fr 1fr` does not convey the full compositional weight of seeing that golden ratio embedded in a complete page with surrounding sections, responsive breakpoints, border systems, and content. The gestalt of "what a mature spatial page looks like" requires seeing one, not reading about one.

**What is lost:** The perceptual calibration. The exploration-era builder knew what "good" looked like because they had seen 24 examples of it. The single-session builder must invent "good" from scratch, guided only by verbal descriptions. This is like learning to paint from a textbook without ever seeing a painting.

### Component C: Content-Form Mapping Experience

**What it was:** The exploration-era builders had practiced mapping content to spatial form across multiple articles and dimensions. AD-006's builder was not the first to attempt content-topology formation -- it was the builder who attempted it after DD builders had explored density, OD builders had explored organization, and the project had accumulated months of content-spatial reasoning. The SKILL of translating "this article converges five threads" into "the page should physically narrow" had been practiced, even if by different agents.

**Can curated research substitute?** NO. This is the hardest gap to fill. Content-form mapping is a SKILL, not knowledge. You cannot teach it by describing it. The curated research can say "map content structure to spatial form" but the builder must PERFORM the mapping, and performing it well requires having done it before (or being given enough time to develop the skill in-session).

**What is lost:** The speed of hypothesis formation. An experienced mapper leaps from "comparison section" to "asymmetric columns" in seconds. A first-time mapper dwells at the generic level ("comparison = side-by-side columns") and may never reach the content-specific level ("comparison of paradigms where one wins = asymmetric columns with the winner in the wider column"). The curated research can TRY to model this reasoning chain, but reading a chain and performing it are different.

### Component D: Project-Specific Conventions

**What it was:** AD-CONVENTION-SPEC.md (822 lines), border category systems, offset grammars, responsive strategies, spacing tokens. These were DISCOVERED during building and codified as conventions. The builder inherited them as a consistent visual language.

**Can curated research substitute?** YES, largely. Conventions are explicit, documented rules. The convention compiler (Phase 2, Agent 2C) can provide binary constraints. The builder may not understand WHY the three-tier border system exists (discovered through building 7 sections), but it can follow the spec. This is the one component where curated research is a genuine substitute.

**What is lost:** The sense of ownership. When you BUILD a border system and discover it works across sections, you understand it at a depth that specification-following cannot match. But for practical purposes, following the spec produces compliant output.

### Component E: Human Curation Feedback

**What it was:** The exploration project had active human direction. The user chose which explorations to build, which to enrich, which findings to promote, and which architectural decisions to make. This human judgment steered the creative direction.

**Can curated research substitute?** PARTIALLY. The architecture replaces human curation with automated evaluation agents. These can assess falsifiable criteria and provide prescriptive feedback. But they cannot provide TASTE -- the human judgment that "this page's hub-spoke feels right but the bento section is forced." Automated evaluators assess measurable properties. The human assessed ineffable ones.

**What is lost:** Direction. The builder receives evaluation feedback that is diagnostic ("this section scores 3/10 on content-form coupling") but not directional ("try making the hub-spoke the gravitational center instead of a side panel"). The curated research can provide direction for the first pass, but BUILD-2 corrections are driven by automated feedback, which is diagnostic, not creative.

### Component F: Evaluation Calibration

**What it was:** The exploration project iterated across 6 waves, with each wave's evaluation informing the next wave's building. The quality standard was established empirically -- "this is what 10/10 looks like" because AD-006 existed as a reference.

**Can curated research substitute?** NO. The single-session builder has no internal calibration for what "10/10" looks like. The evaluation agents have the 10 falsifiable criteria, but these are threshold-based (pass/fail), not gradient-based (how far above the threshold is excellence). A builder can produce a page that scores 7/10 and not know whether it should aim higher because it has no reference for what 9/10 looks like.

**What is lost:** Ambition calibration. The exploration-era builders saw AD-006 and knew what was possible. The single-session builder has no such reference and may stop at "competent" when "extraordinary" was achievable.

### Summary Table

| Component | Substitutability | What is Lost |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| A: CSS vocabulary breadth | PARTIAL (20-40 of 337) | Combinatorial range for novel compositions |
| B: Design pattern familiarity | POOR | Perceptual calibration ("what good looks like") |
| C: Content-form mapping experience | NONE | Speed and depth of spatial hypothesis formation |
| D: Project-specific conventions | GOOD | Sense of ownership (functional output unaffected) |
| E: Human curation feedback | PARTIAL | Creative direction beyond diagnostic assessment |
| F: Evaluation calibration | NONE | Ambition calibration ("what's possible") |

## 2. Phase Transition Dependency Analysis

The 4 minimum conditions from Report #2 are:

1. **Unframed reading** -- the builder reads the article without procedural framing
2. **Spatial vocabulary available** -- curated research provides spatial techniques
3. **CSS writing** -- the builder writes CSS that forces topology crystallization
4. **30+ minutes** of combined content engagement and building

### Which conditions DEPEND on accumulated context?

**Condition 1 (Unframed reading): NO dependency.** This is an architectural decision, not a context-dependent capability. The proposed architecture places the article first and asks for personal spatial impressions before showing the structural analysis. This condition is fully achievable in a single session. It requires prompt design, not accumulated context.

**Condition 2 (Spatial vocabulary available): PARTIAL dependency.** The curated research provides vocabulary. But the RICHNESS of that vocabulary is diminished compared to the exploration era. A builder with 337 findings can project more spatial possibilities onto content than a builder with 20-40 curated techniques. The critical question: is 20-40 techniques ENOUGH to activate the spatial projection step?

Evidence from the depth curve suggests yes, but with a caveat. Report #8 identifies that Phase 1->2 transition (recognition to hypotheses) requires reading the article "with spatial vocabulary available." The key word is "available," not "extensive." A builder who knows 20 spatial techniques can form hypotheses. But the hypotheses will draw from a smaller palette. The difference is not binary (hypotheses vs. no hypotheses) but combinatorial (fewer unique spatial possibilities).

**However:** "Spatial vocabulary available" may specifically require having BUILT with that vocabulary before, not just having read about it. This is the critical open question. The exploration-era builders had not just read about golden ratio grids -- they had seen them work. Report #7 documents that the cognitive precondition for Invention #2 (golden ratio columns) is "mathematical awareness + CSS Grid fluency + content understanding." CSS Grid fluency includes the tactile knowledge that `fr` accepts decimals and that `1.618fr` feels different from `2fr`. A builder who has never typed `1.618fr` may not project it as a possibility.

**My assessment:** Reading about spatial techniques is SUFFICIENT to activate Phase 2 (hypotheses), but the hypotheses will be less specific and less confident than those formed by an experienced builder. The curated research must include IMPLEMENTATION-LEVEL detail (actual CSS snippets, not just technique names) to partially compensate for the lack of building experience.

**Condition 3 (CSS writing): NO dependency.** CSS writing is a mechanical capability. An Opus-class model can write CSS regardless of accumulated context. The topology crystallizes through the ACT of writing, not through prior writing experience. The grid area names that force content-spatial integration (`grid-template-areas: "spiral-wide spiral-wide spiral-wide"`) produce the same cognitive effect whether this is the builder's first or hundredth grid.

**But:** The QUALITY of the initial CSS is affected. An experienced builder starts with more sophisticated spatial structures. A first-time builder may start with simpler structures that take longer to evolve into topology-carrying forms. The Phase 2->3 transition takes longer if the initial CSS is generic.

**Condition 4 (30+ minutes): INCREASED dependency.** The 30-minute threshold was estimated from exploration-era builds where the builder had accumulated context that accelerated hypothesis formation. A single-session builder without accumulated context may need MORE time for the same transition. Specifically:

- Exploration-era: 0-15 min content scanning (fast because they had seen similar content types before) -> 15-30 min hypotheses (fast because 337 findings available for projection) -> 30-60 min topology
- Single-session: 0-15 min content scanning (same speed) -> 15-40 min hypotheses (SLOWER because smaller vocabulary) -> 40-70 min topology

**My estimate:** The single-session builder needs 40-50 minutes to reach Phase 3, not 30 minutes. The proposed 50-minute BUILD-1 window is tight but potentially sufficient IF the builder begins writing CSS at minute 15 (after reading).

### Dependency Summary

| Condition | Depends on Accumulated Context? | Impact |
|-----------|-------------------------------|--------|
| 1. Unframed reading | NO | Achievable by architecture design |
| 2. Spatial vocabulary | PARTIALLY | Narrower hypothesis palette; needs implementation-level research |
| 3. CSS writing | NO mechanically; YES qualitatively | Slower evolution from generic to topology-carrying CSS |
| 4. 30+ minutes | INCREASED NEED | Estimate 40-50 min vs 30 min for exploration-era builders |

## 3. The Most Likely Failure Scenario: Phase 2 Stall

### What Phase 2 Stall Looks Like

The builder reads the article, reads the curated research, forms hypotheses, and begins writing CSS. But the CSS remains at the GENERIC level. The builder produces a page that is structurally organized (it has different sections with different layouts) but not content-coupled (the layouts could be swapped between sections without losing meaning).

**Concrete Phase 2 output example (hypothetical):**

```css
/* Phase 2 builder writes: */
.section-comparison {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr;  /* symmetric because "comparison = side-by-side" */
  gap: 32px;
}
.section-deep-dive {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-columns: 3fr 1fr;  /* asymmetric because "main content + sidebar" */
  gap: 24px;
}
.section-synthesis {
  max-width: 720px;
  margin: 0 auto;  /* centered single-column because "synthesis = convergence" */
}
```

**What Phase 3 output looks like for the same content:**

```css
/* Phase 3 builder writes: */
.section-paradigm-clash {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-areas: "established displacement";
  grid-template-columns: 1fr 1.618fr;  /* the new paradigm gets golden ratio emphasis because it's WINNING */
}
.section-evidence-spiral {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-areas:
    "spiral-wide     spiral-wide     spiral-wide"
    "spiral-medium   spiral-medium   spiral-narrow"
    "spiral-deep     spiral-deep     spiral-narrow";
  grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr 1.618fr;
}
.section-synthesis-hub {
  display: grid;
  grid-template-areas:
    "thread-a  convergence  thread-b"
    "thread-c  convergence  thread-d";
  grid-template-columns: 1fr 1.618fr 1fr;  /* convergence point IS the hub */
}
```

### The Diagnostic Differences

| Signal | Phase 2 | Phase 3 |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Grid area names | Generic (`left`, `right`, `main`, `sidebar`) or absent | Content-semantic (`paradigm-clash`, `convergence`, `thread-a`) |
| Proportions | Generic ratios (`1fr 1fr`, `3fr 1fr`) | Content-justified ratios (`1.618fr` with reasoning) |
| Layout justification | Based on CONTENT TYPE ("comparison = 2 columns") | Based on CONTENT MEANING ("this comparison has a winner = asymmetric") |
| Layout diversity | 2-3 patterns reused across sections | Section-specific layouts (each section has its own grid) |
| Comments | Describe WHAT the layout is ("comparison grid") | Describe WHY the layout serves THIS content ("winner paradigm gets wider column") |
| The "swap test" | Swapping layouts between sections loses nothing | Swapping would lose meaning (the winning paradigm in the narrow column contradicts the content) |

### Can Evaluators Distinguish Phase 2 from Phase 3?

**YES, using these 5 signals:**

1. **Grid-template-areas presence and naming:** Phase 2 uses generic column divisions or absent named areas. Phase 3 uses content-vocabulary named areas. This is binary and unambiguous.

2. **The swap test:** Ask the evaluator to mentally swap two section layouts. If the swap loses no content meaning -> Phase 2. If the swap damages an argument -> Phase 3.

3. **Asymmetric proportions with content justification:** Phase 2 uses asymmetry for generic reasons (main/sidebar). Phase 3 uses asymmetry to ENCODE content hierarchy (the winning paradigm is wider).

4. **Comment content:** Phase 2 comments describe layout structure. Phase 3 comments explain content-spatial reasoning.

5. **Self-referential components:** Phase 2 produces zero self-referential components. Phase 3 begins producing them (the page starts noticing its own structure).

An evaluator checking these 5 signals can distinguish Phase 2 from Phase 3 with high confidence. The critical test is #2 (the swap test): it is the hardest to game and the most diagnostic.

### The Phase 2 Stall Cascade

If the builder stalls at Phase 2, the following cascade occurs:

1. BUILD-1 output scores 4-6/10 on falsifiable criteria (some asymmetric proportions, some diversity, but no content-form coupling)
2. EVALUATE-1 identifies the lack of coupling but provides diagnostic feedback, not creative direction
3. BUILD-2 receives feedback like "Section 4's comparison grid should be asymmetric" but applies it MECHANICALLY (changes `1fr 1fr` to `2fr 1fr` without re-engaging with the content)
4. BUILD-2 output scores 5-7/10 -- cosmetic improvement, not structural deepening
5. The architecture has spent $91-101 to produce a 6/10 page -- worse value than the $30 pipeline at 0/10 (where 0/10 is at least expected)

This is the architecture's worst outcome: expensive mediocrity.

## 4. Design Interventions for Reliability

### Intervention 1: Include 2-3 Exemplar Completed Pages

**The proposal:** The curated research package includes 2-3 COMPLETE pages (or substantial excerpts) from the exploration era, not as templates but as demonstrations of what content-driven spatial design looks like.

**Why it helps:** This directly addresses Component B (design pattern familiarity) and Component F (evaluation calibration). The builder SEES what Phase 3 output looks like. It sees named grid areas, golden ratio proportions, self-referential components, and content-mapped color semantics IN CONTEXT. This is the difference between reading a description of a painting and seeing the painting.

**The risk:** The builder may copy spatial forms rather than inventing them. If the exemplar has a hub-spoke section, the builder may default to hub-spoke for any synthesis section, even if the content demands something different.

**Mitigation:** Include exemplars from DIFFERENT articles and DIFFERENT content types. Frame them explicitly: "These pages are examples of content-driven spatial design. The spatial forms in them were invented for THEIR content. Your content demands its OWN spatial forms."

**My assessment:** This is the HIGHEST-IMPACT intervention. The perceptual calibration it provides -- showing the builder what Phase 3 output LOOKS like -- addresses the deepest gap in the single-session context. Without exemplars, the builder must invent "good" from verbal description alone.

### Intervention 2: Include Process Traces in Curated Research

**The proposal:** The curated research includes not just OUTPUTS (spatial techniques) but REASONING TRACES: "The AD-006 builder read about progressive depth, formed the hypothesis that the page should physically narrow, and realized that golden ratio columns (1fr 1fr 1.618fr) create this narrowing because 1.618 is the proportion where subordination feels natural."

**Why it helps:** This models the content-form mapping SKILL (Component C). The builder does not just learn WHAT golden ratio columns are -- it learns HOW the chain from content meaning to spatial form works. The reasoning trace is a worked example of the cognitive process the builder needs to perform.

**The risk:** The builder may follow the reasoning trace mechanically rather than forming its own associations. If the trace says "progressive depth -> narrowing -> golden ratio," the builder may apply golden ratio to ANY progressive structure, even when a different spatial form would better serve the specific content.

**Mitigation:** Include 3-4 reasoning traces for DIFFERENT content-to-spatial mappings. Show that the same mechanism (e.g., asymmetric columns) can be justified for DIFFERENT content reasons (paradigm dominance, progressive depth, gravitational synthesis). This demonstrates that the PROCESS is general, even though each application is content-specific.

**My assessment:** HIGH IMPACT, but only if the traces are genuinely diverse. A single trace teaches imitation. Four traces teach the SKILL of mapping.

### Intervention 3: "Build the Section You Feel Strongest About First"

**The proposal:** Instead of "build zone by zone from top to bottom," the BUILD-1 prompt says "start with the ONE section that most excites you spatially."

**Why it helps:** Phase 3 topology forms THROUGH building. If the builder starts with its strongest spatial hypothesis, it reaches Phase 3 faster because the first CSS decisions are content-driven rather than procedural. Starting from the top forces the builder to make spatial decisions for the header and introduction -- often the LEAST spatially interesting sections -- before it has formed a topology.

**The risk:** The builder may produce a single brilliant section and then lose steam for the remaining sections, defaulting to generic layouts.

**Mitigation:** After building the first section, the prompt says: "Now that you have established the page's spatial voice, extend it to the other sections. Each section's spatial logic should be different but RELATED to the spatial voice you established."

**My assessment:** MODERATE IMPACT. This accelerates the Phase 2->3 transition but does not fundamentally change whether it occurs. A builder that was going to stall at Phase 2 will still stall -- it will just stall on a section it chose rather than one imposed by order.

### Intervention 4: Phase 3 Detector Triggering Additional Build Time

**The proposal:** After BUILD-1, a lightweight detection agent checks the output for Phase 3 signals (see Section 5 below). If Phase 2 is detected, the architecture triggers an ADDITIONAL build session with modified prompting designed to push the builder past the transition.

**Why it helps:** This directly addresses the Phase 2 stall cascade. Instead of feeding Phase 2 output into the standard evaluation-deepening loop (which produces cosmetic patching), the detector routes to a RECOVERY path designed for the specific failure.

**The recovery prompt would be:**

```
Your BUILD-1 output shows strong structural organization but the layouts
are not yet coupled to the CONTENT's specific meaning. Here is a test:

For each section, I swapped the layout with an adjacent section's layout.
In none of the cases did the swap damage the content's argument.

This means the layouts are DECORATING the content, not EMBODYING it.

Pick your 2 strongest sections. For EACH:
1. Re-read the section in the article
2. Write 2 sentences about what this section ARGUES, not what it contains
3. Ask: what spatial form would MAKE that argument visible?
4. Rebuild the section's CSS from your answer

You have 30 additional minutes. Focus on depth, not breadth.
```

**My assessment:** HIGHEST IMPACT for preventing the worst-case outcome. This is the difference between an architecture that fails gracefully and one that produces expensive mediocrity. The Phase 3 detector is cheap ($2-3 for a lightweight analysis agent) and the recovery path directly targets the failure mechanism.

### Intervention 5: Dual-Builder Architecture for Critical Builds

**The proposal:** For Tier A (maximum effort) builds, spawn TWO independent builders with the same inputs. After BUILD-1, compare their outputs. Select the builder that achieved a deeper phase transition and continue with that builder for BUILD-2.

**Why it helps:** The phase transition has inherent variance (Report #6, FM-9). Two independent attempts double the probability that at least one builder reaches Phase 3. If P(Phase 3 | single builder) = 0.6, then P(Phase 3 | at least one of two builders) = 1 - (1-0.6)^2 = 0.84.

**The cost:** Doubles the BUILD-1 cost ($22 -> $44). But if a Phase 2 stall wastes the remaining $69 of the architecture, spending $22 extra to prevent it is justified.

**My assessment:** MODERATE IMPACT per dollar. It addresses variance but not the systematic gap. If the fundamental probability of Phase 3 is low (e.g., 0.3), two builders only raise it to 0.51. If the fundamental probability is moderate (0.6), two builders raise it to 0.84 -- a meaningful improvement.

## 5. Phase Transition Detection: 7 Binary Signals

An evaluation agent can check BUILD-1 output for these signals to determine whether the builder reached Phase 3 or stalled at Phase 2.

### Signal 1: Content-Vocabulary Grid Area Names
**What to check:** Search the CSS for `grid-template-areas` declarations. Extract area names.
**How to check:** Parse all grid-template-areas values. Check whether area names reference content concepts (article vocabulary) or generic layout concepts (position/role vocabulary).
**Phase 2:** Area names like `"main sidebar"`, `"left right"`, `"header content footer"`, or absent entirely (using grid-template-columns only).
**Phase 3:** Area names like `"paradigm-old paradigm-new"`, `"evidence synthesis"`, `"spiral-wide spiral-narrow"` -- names drawn from the article's vocabulary.
**Binary test:** Are 2+ grid-template-areas declarations present with content-vocabulary names? YES = Phase 3 signal. NO = Phase 2.

### Signal 2: Asymmetric Grid Proportions with Justification
**What to check:** Search for `grid-template-columns` declarations with non-equal proportions.
**How to check:** Parse all grid-template-columns values. Identify any that are NOT `1fr 1fr`, `repeat(N, 1fr)`, or fixed-width sidebars. Check adjacent CSS comments for content-based reasoning.
**Phase 2:** All grids are symmetric, or asymmetry is generic (`3fr 1fr` for main/sidebar without content justification).
**Phase 3:** Asymmetric proportions with content-driven values (1.618fr, or specific ratios chosen for meaning) and comments explaining the content reason.
**Binary test:** Are 2+ non-symmetric grid formulas present? YES = Phase 3 signal. NO = Phase 2.

### Signal 3: The Swap Test (at least one section)
**What to check:** For each section with a distinctive layout, ask: would swapping this layout with an adjacent section's layout damage the content's argument?
**How to check:** The evaluator mentally (or textually) describes what happens if Section A's grid is applied to Section B's content. Does the argument suffer?
**Phase 2:** No section's layout is coupled to its content. All swaps are neutral.
**Phase 3:** At least 2 sections where the swap would damage meaning (e.g., the winning paradigm would end up in the narrow column, or the progressive depth section would lose its narrowing effect).
**Binary test:** Do 2+ sections fail the swap test (swap damages meaning)? YES = Phase 3 signal. NO = Phase 2.

### Signal 4: Self-Referential Component Presence
**What to check:** Search for components that reference the page's own structure, position, or density.
**How to check:** Look for: (a) components that visually represent the page's sections or layout, (b) scroll progress indicators, (c) fractal/meta annotations, (d) components that reference the page's own patterns.
**Phase 2:** Zero self-referential components. The page describes the article but does not describe itself.
**Phase 3:** At least 1 self-referential component (the builder noticed that the page is about what the page does).
**Binary test:** Is 1+ self-referential component present? YES = Phase 3 signal. NO = Phase 2.

### Signal 5: CSS Comments Reference Content Reasoning
**What to check:** Sample 10 CSS comments from the structural/layout sections.
**How to check:** Classify each comment as "content-reasoning" (explains why a spatial choice serves the article's argument) or "rule-compliance" / "descriptive" (names the layout pattern or references constraints).
**Phase 2:** 0-2 of 10 comments are content-reasoning. Most describe what the layout IS ("comparison grid") or reference compliance ("soul-compliant border").
**Phase 3:** 5+ of 10 comments are content-reasoning. Comments explain WHY a spatial choice was made for THIS content ("hub gets golden ratio because synthesis is the gravitational center of the argument").
**Binary test:** Are 5+ of 10 sampled comments content-reasoning? YES = Phase 3 signal. NO = Phase 2.

### Signal 6: Layout Diversity Count
**What to check:** Count the number of fundamentally different layout systems in the page.
**How to check:** Count distinct grid-template-areas definitions, distinct flex configurations used for section-level layout, and distinct positioning compositions. Variations of the same pattern (e.g., two 2-column grids with different proportions) count as ONE system.
**Phase 2:** 1-2 distinct layout systems. The builder uses one spatial idea (centered column + sidebar) across the whole page, with minor variations.
**Phase 3:** 4+ distinct layout systems. Each section has its own spatial logic because the builder understood that each section makes a different KIND of argument.
**Binary test:** Are 4+ distinct layout systems present? YES = Phase 3 signal. NO = Phase 2.

### Signal 7: Non-Default Reading Direction or Positioned Composition
**What to check:** Search for `writing-mode` (non-default), `position: sticky` or `position: fixed` (beyond skip-link), or `transform` used for layout direction (skewX, rotate for layout purposes).
**How to check:** Grep the CSS for these declarations and verify they serve a content purpose.
**Phase 2:** Zero non-default reading directions, zero positioned compositions beyond skip-link.
**Phase 3:** At least 1 non-default reading direction or 1 positioned composition pair (relative/absolute for spatial depth).
**Binary test:** Is 1+ non-default direction or positioned composition present? YES = Phase 3 signal. NO = Phase 2.

### Scoring

**Phase 3 confident:** 5-7 signals YES (the transition clearly occurred)
**Phase 3 marginal:** 3-4 signals YES (partial transition; topology forming but incomplete)
**Phase 2 stall:** 0-2 signals YES (the transition did not occur; trigger recovery path)

The detector costs approximately $2-3 (a lightweight evaluation agent reading the HTML/CSS and checking these 7 binary signals). It adds 5-10 minutes to the pipeline but prevents the $69 waste of continuing with Phase 2 output.

## 6. Risk Quantification

### The Probability Estimate

**P(Phase 3 | curated research + structural analysis + 50 min build time) = 0.45-0.55**

### Reasoning

**Factors that INCREASE probability (vs. the pipeline's ~0):**

1. Content-first reading order (eliminates the single most destructive constraint C-06 and the mandated reading order C-01/02/03): +25%
2. Spatial vocabulary available from curated research (even though narrower than 337 findings, it activates spatial projection): +15%
3. 50 minutes of build time (sufficient for the 40-50 minute adjusted Phase 3 threshold): +10%
4. Hypothesis-driven framing (the structural analysis from Phase 3 DISCOVER agent primes spatial reasoning): +10%
5. No structural caps or deployment quotas (the builder can implement as many spatial ideas as the content demands): +5%

**Factors that DECREASE probability (vs. exploration-era ~0.9):**

1. No accumulated CSS vocabulary beyond curated selection (narrower hypothesis palette): -15%
2. No design pattern familiarity (never SEEN a complete content-driven page): -20%
3. No content-form mapping experience (performing the skill for the first time): -15%
4. No human curation feedback (automated evaluation is diagnostic, not creative): -5%
5. No perceptual calibration for what "good" looks like: -10%

**Baseline:** The exploration-era probability of Phase 3 was approximately 0.85-0.95 (almost all crown jewel builds achieved it, given sufficient conditions). Starting from 0.90 and applying the decreases: 0.90 - 0.15 - 0.20 - 0.15 - 0.05 - 0.10 = 0.25. But the increases from architectural changes (which the exploration era also had): the baseline should be the PIPELINE's probability (~0.00) with the increases applied: 0.00 + 0.25 + 0.15 + 0.10 + 0.10 + 0.05 = 0.65.

**Reconciled estimate:** The two approaches (top-down from exploration, bottom-up from pipeline) bracket the probability at 0.25-0.65. The midpoint is 0.45. Adjusting upward slightly because an Opus builder with 50 minutes and spatial vocabulary has significant capacity even without accumulated context: **0.45-0.55**.

### What This Means for the Architecture

A 50% probability of Phase 3 means:
- **Half of $91-101 builds will produce 7-9/10 output** (extraordinary value)
- **Half will produce 5-6/10 output** (expensive mediocrity)
- **Expected quality per dollar: worse than the reformed pipeline** unless interventions raise the probability

### With Interventions Applied

| Scenario | P(Phase 3) | Expected Quality |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Base architecture | 0.45-0.55 | 6.0/10 average |
| + Exemplar pages (Intervention 1) | 0.55-0.65 | 6.5/10 average |
| + Process traces (Intervention 2) | 0.60-0.70 | 7.0/10 average |
| + Phase 3 detector + recovery (Intervention 4) | 0.70-0.80 | 7.5/10 average |
| + Dual builders (Intervention 5) | 0.80-0.90 | 8.0/10 average |
